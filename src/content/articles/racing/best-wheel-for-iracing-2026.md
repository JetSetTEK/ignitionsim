## Why iRacing has a torque ceiling around 12Nm

iRacing's tire model is the most communicative in the business, but it's not asking for arm-wrestling forces. Across every class — Mazda MX-5, GT4, GT3, the open-wheel cars — the information that makes you fast lives well under 12Nm. Past that you're buying *headroom* (so force spikes don't clip) and *heavy-car realism* (older no-power-steering cars), not raw pace.

That's why the community consensus has hardened: **9-12Nm is the sweet spot, and 12Nm is the practical ceiling.** A [MOZA R12](/racing/gear/moza-r12) at 12Nm has enough for everything iRacing throws at you. The drivers winning splits aren't on 21Nm flagships because they're faster — they're on whatever they're consistent on, run *below* max, tuned to never clip. It's the same truth we lay out in [the 2026 wheelbase ranking](/racing/best-sim-racing-wheelbases-2026): torque buys headroom, not lap time.

<div class="callout tip">

Set Wheel Force to your base's real peak (12Nm on an R12), map a button to Auto, drive two clean laps, and hit it once per car. iRacing scales the tire forces into whatever torque your base actually has.</p>

## The rim: don't ignore the half you actually touch

The base makes the force; the rim makes you fast on race day. This is where the open-wheel-vs-GT choice actually lives, and most buyers get it backwards by fixating on the base.

**For iRacing road and GT racing, buy a round GT-style rim.** You want:

- A **round GT-style rim**, not a bare formula plate — round gives you better hand placement for long stints and mixed-class endurance.
- **At least two rotary encoders** for on-the-fly brake bias, traction control and fuel/ABS maps. Reaching for a keyboard mid-race is how you lose positions.
- Enough buttons for pit limiter, black-box cycling, and headlights for multi-class racing.

**For dedicated open-wheel, a flat formula rim earns its keep.** A formula plate with a built-in display and a dense button matrix matches the low, arms-out formula driving position and puts every control at your thumbs — genuinely better for the Formula and open-wheel series. But it's the *worse* all-rounder: if you race a GT3 on Tuesday and an F-car on Thursday, the round GT rim wins overall. Buy the formula rim only if open-wheel is genuinely your main event.

A great base with a button-starved rim is a fast car with no dashboard. Whichever direction you go, a rim with rotaries and a screen is the upgrade people notice a week in.

## Do you still need irFFB? (2026 answer: probably not)

For years the standard iRacing advice was to run irFFB, the third-party layer that upsampled iRacing's 60Hz force feedback for smoother detail on direct-drive bases. **As of the 2024 Season 2 update, that changed.** iRacing added a native 360Hz FFB output — it sends six 60Hz packets together to deliver finer road texture, kerb and surface detail — and for direct-drive bases that native feed has caught up enough that **irFFB is no longer mandatory.** Most DD iRacers now run native and never install it.

The nuance: the 360Hz feed adds a little latency (roughly 16ms) because of how the packets are bundled, and irFFB still helps *belt and gear* users squeeze extra grip cues out of the old 60Hz signal. But if you're buying a modern DD base for iRacing, plan on native FFB — set Wheel Force to your peak Nm, run Auto per car, and only reach for irFFB if you specifically want to filter or reshape the signal. Our [full FFB settings guide](/racing/best-ffb-settings-iracing-acc-lmu-2026) walks the per-base numbers.

## The setup tax nobody quotes you

- **Rim swaps and quick releases.** If you'll run both a GT and a formula rim, your quick release matters. MOZA's QR and Simagic's open QR-A make swapping painless; Fanatec's QR2 can *disable force feedback on non-Fanatec rims*, so budget for Fanatec rims if you buy into that ecosystem. Confirm your rim plan before the base.
- **Mounting.** A 12Nm R12 or 18Nm Evo Pro needs a 4-bolt mount and a rigid stand or 8020 rig, not a desk clamp — a big base on a flexing desk kills the very detail you paid for.
- **FFB settings do more than torque.** A badly tuned 12Nm base feels worse than a well-tuned 9Nm one. Run Auto per car, watch for clipping, and read the FFB guide before you blame the hardware.
- **Wheel-vs-base priority.** Once you're in the 9-12Nm sweet spot, the next dollar goes to the rim (rotaries, screen) and pedals — not a bigger base.

## What the community gets right (and wrong)

The forums are **right that pedals matter as much as the wheel** — a consistent load-cell brake wins more iRacing time than another 6Nm of torque, which is exactly why we say [pedals over power](/racing/pedals-over-power). They're **right that 12Nm is enough** and that MOZA's software is a genuine advantage.

Where they overreach: the **brand tribalism.** Threads turn into MOZA-vs-Fanatec-vs-Simagic holy wars when, at 9-12Nm, all three deliver excellent, race-winning FFB. The differences are real but small; the *ecosystem, rim plan and console path* should decide it, not which brand a stranger evangelizes. The other myth is that **you need a flagship to compete** — you don't. iRacing splits are won on tuning and seat time, not the spec sheet.

## Who should skip the upgrade

If you're on a recent belt wheel and racing the lower splits casually, you don't *need* a new base to enjoy iRacing — spend the money on a [proper rig or solid stand](/racing/desk-vs-cockpit-sim-racing-2026) and a load-cell pedal set first, since a flexing mount and a numb brake cost you more than belt drive does. And if you're chasing podiums but your FFB feels flat, the fix is usually free: read the [FFB settings guide](/racing/best-ffb-settings-iracing-acc-lmu-2026) before you spend a dollar.

## The buy order

1. Confirm iRacing is PC-only for you — if you also game on console, that decision (Fanatec/Logitech) comes first.
2. Buy torque in the sweet spot: 9-12Nm covers every iRacing class. Don't chase 21Nm.
3. Pick your rim by discipline — round GT rim with rotaries for road/GT, flat formula rim only if open-wheel is your main event.
4. Budget a 4-bolt mount and rigid stand in the same cart as any 12Nm+ base.
5. Spend leftover money on a load-cell brake before more torque.
6. Run native 360Hz FFB, set Wheel Force to peak Nm, and hit Auto on every car.
