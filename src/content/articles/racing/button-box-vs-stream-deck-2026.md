Verdict first: **if you race in VR, buy a physical button box — nothing else works in a headset. If you race on a screen you can see, a Stream Deck is the more flexible buy for most people, and the Stream Deck + even claws back the rotary dials a box used to win on.** And if your wheel still has empty buttons, the honest answer this month is *neither* — you're not out of inputs yet.

There's a point in every sim racer's life where the wheel runs out of buttons. You're in an endurance race juggling brake bias, traction control, ABS, fuel mix, lights, wipers, the pit limiter and three MFD pages, and you've got eleven inputs to map fifteen functions onto. That's when people start shopping for a **button box** or a **Stream Deck** — and the internet immediately tells them two opposite things. The good news: the decision is mostly settled by one variable. Let me give you the rule first, then the detail, because if you race in VR the rest of this article barely matters.

## The rule: it's about whether you can see the screen

A **Stream Deck** is a grid of small LCD keys whose labels change with context. Its entire superpower is **visual** — the keys show you what they do, and they re-label themselves per car and per game. Take away your ability to look at it and you've thrown away the whole point.

So:

- **If you race in VR**, you **cannot see a Stream Deck**, full stop. A headset blanks out anything in the real room, and every guide that has actually tried it says the same thing: users in a headset "can't see a Stream Deck's labels or a touchscreen at all." Muscle memory on **physical switches** — a button box you find by feel — is the only thing that works. **Buy a button box.**
- **If you race on triple monitors or a single screen**, you can glance at a Stream Deck, and its context labels become a real advantage over memorising what an unlabeled toggle does. **A Stream Deck is genuinely compelling.**

That one fork decides it for most people. Everything below is for the buyers on the fence or the ones who want both.

## What each one is actually good at

| | Button box | Stream Deck |
|---|---|---|
| **Find blind (VR)** | Yes — physical switches | No — screen invisible in headset |
| **Rotary encoders** | Yes (brake bias, TC, ABS dials) | Only on the Stream Deck + (4 dials) |
| **Toggle / latching switches** | Yes (ignition, pumps, lights) | No (momentary press only) |
| **Number of functions** | Fixed by physical count | Near-unlimited via pages/folders |
| **Per-car / per-game labels** | No (static engraving or memory) | Yes — changes contextually |
| **Tactile confirmation** | Strong — you feel the click | Weak — flat key press |
| **SimHub / telemetry on keys** | Via mapped inputs; some boxes add a screen | Deep two-way telemetry via plugin |
| **Best for** | VR, dials, switch feel | Visible screens, macros, info display |

The **button box** wins on the things that benefit from being a real, physical control: **rotary encoders** for stepped functions (brake bias up/down, TC level, ABS, fuel mix) and **latching toggle switches** for on/off states (ignition, fuel pump, lights). A real detented dial you can spin without looking is simply better for adjusting brake bias mid-stint than tapping a flat key, and a covered toggle *stays* where you flick it.

The **Stream Deck** wins on **flexibility and information**: more functions than any box via pages and folders, labels that rewrite themselves so you're never guessing, and deep **SimHub** integration that can display live telemetry — a tyre key that flips colour when the fronts are cold, a pit-limiter key that lights when it's active — right on the keys. The one asterisk to "a deck has no dials": the **Stream Deck +** adds four physical rotary dials and a touch strip, which narrows the box's oldest advantage considerably.

## Which model: matching the deck (or box) to how you race

If you land on the Stream Deck side, the model matters more than people expect, and bigger isn't automatically better:

- **Stream Deck MK.2** (15 keys, ~£150) is the default. Fifteen keys covers a startup folder plus a full pit set without paging, and it's the one most sim racers actually end up on.
- **Stream Deck Neo** (8 keys, ~£100) is the budget entry; two switchable pages give you an effective 16 buttons.
- **Stream Deck +** (8 keys + 4 rotary dials + touch strip, ~£200) is the interesting one for racing specifically, because those four dials handle brake bias and TC the way a button box's encoders do.
- **Stream Deck XL** (32 keys, ~£230) is overkill for most, but earns its place if you run multiple cars and sims and want every macro visible at once.

Early in 2026 Elgato also announced the **Stream Deck + XL**, expanding the XL to 36 buttons and adding a touch strip plus six dials — the most button-plus-dial hardware Elgato has shipped, and worth watching if you want everything on one panel. Check current availability rather than assuming it's on shelves.

If you land on the button-box side, you're choosing between DIY, budget pre-built, and premium replica. A few reference points from the current market (verify pricing before you buy — this tier moves and many are small-shop builds):

- **RaceBox GT4 / GT-FLAG** (~$134 / ~$169) — carbon-fibre budget boxes with rotary encoders, toggles and, on the GT-FLAG, an RGB matrix display.
- **RaceBox GTE** (~$254) — steps up to an integrated 5" telemetry display with SimHub compatibility out of the box, blurring the line between "box" and "deck."
- **ReaperWare 32-Elite** (~£130) — 20 LED push buttons, two knobs, three toggles, 32 customisable inputs; plug-and-play value.
- **Precision Sim Engineering Pro Switch Panel** (~$250) — 20 RGB-lit buttons plus joysticks, RAM/VESA mountable.
- **ignitiON Controls Mini RR** (~£399) and **GRID / Apex** replica boxes — the premium, real-automotive-switch end for buyers who want the look and feel of a genuine GT3 panel.

## The hybrid: Race Deck and running both

You don't actually have to choose. A **Race Deck** is just a Stream Deck mounted in a sim-racing case — Apex Engineering and RaceBox both make them — so it bolts neatly to your rig instead of sliding around the desk. Same hardware, same software, cleaner integration; the good ones are compatible across Stream Deck revisions.

And plenty of serious rigs run **both**: a button box for the rotaries and toggles your hands need to find blind, and a Stream Deck for the long tail of macros, telemetry pages and rarely-used functions. If your wheel already carries the core racing inputs and you're only adding panels for the overflow, a small box plus a deck covers nearly everything — though at that point you're well past the "out of buttons" threshold and into enthusiast territory.

## When to buy either one (and when not to)

Here's the honest part: **most people don't need either yet.** A modern GT wheel like the [MOZA RS V2](/racing/gear/moza-rs-v2) or [Fanatec McLaren GT3](/racing/gear/fanatec-mclaren) already carries the buttons, paddles and a rotary or two that cover sprint racing. You add a control panel when you genuinely **run out of inputs** — which in practice means:

- **Endurance racing** with full pit functions, fuel mix and stint management
- **Sims with deep MFDs** (iRacing's black boxes, ACC's pit pages) where you're constantly paging
- **Multi-class or rally** where lights, wipers and stage functions pile on top of the racing basics

If you're a sprint-race-only GT driver, your wheel is enough and the money belongs [further up the upgrade path](/racing/smart-upgrade-path) — on pedals or your base. Buying a Stream Deck "to look pro" while your wheel still has three unmapped buttons is the classic accessory-drawer purchase.

## What the community says

The forums have converged on exactly the VR fork above: VR racers are near-unanimous that a Stream Deck is useless in a headset and a physical box is the answer, while monitor racers rave about Stream Deck labels and SimHub telemetry keys. The other consistent note is from endurance and iRacing crowds — once you're managing pit functions and black boxes, *some* extra control surface stops being optional. The one recurring gotcha nobody warns you about up front is the vJOY overlap between SimHub and the Stream Deck app; when someone's "buttons randomly stopped working," that shared virtual controller is usually why. The bikeshed argument is always button box versus Stream Deck; the real answer is usually "what do you actually run out of, and can you see the screen."

## Warnings before you buy

- **Binding limits.** Some sims cap how many controller buttons or keyboard binds they'll register. Plan your most-used functions onto the surface you can reach fastest, and don't assume 32 keys means 32 usable in-game slots in every title.
- **Mounting.** A Stream Deck loose on the desk slides mid-race; a button box that isn't bolted or clamped down will walk under a hard toggle flick. Budget a Race Deck case or a VESA/RAM mount, not just the panel.
- **Software dependency.** A Stream Deck needs its app running to do anything, and its cleverest tricks (context labels, SimHub telemetry) depend on plugins that occasionally break with updates. A dumb button box just presents as a controller — fewer features, but nothing to crash mid-stint.
- **VR is the hard line.** Do not talk yourself into a Stream Deck "for now" if you race in a headset. You will not see it, and you'll be back buying a box within a month.

## Who should skip both

Sprint-only GT and formula drivers whose wheel still has spare inputs. VR drivers who haven't yet wanted more than their wheel offers (and if you do want more, you already know it's a box, not a deck). Anyone whose budget hasn't yet reached a solid base, [pedals](/racing/pedals-over-power) and a rigid rig — control panels are a refinement, not a foundation.

## The buy order

1. Confirm you're actually out of inputs — count the unmapped buttons on your wheel first.
2. Answer the VR question. Headset → button box, full stop. Visible screen → continue.
3. On a screen, decide macros-and-info (Stream Deck) vs dials-and-toggles (button box). Want dials *and* labels? The Stream Deck + or a screen-equipped box like the RaceBox GTE splits the difference.
4. Pick the size honestly: MK.2 (15 keys) suits most; go XL only if you truly run multiple cars and sims.
5. Budget the mount (Race Deck case, VESA, or clamp) in the same cart as the panel.
6. Map the whole rig in the [racing configurator](/racing/configurator) before you check out.

## The verdict

**Race in VR → buy a button box.** Physical switches you find by feel are the only thing that works in a headset, and rotaries for brake bias and TC are worth it on their own. **Race on a visible screen and love macros or telemetry → a Stream Deck (or rig-mounted Race Deck)** gives you near-unlimited, self-labelling functions a box can't match — and the Stream Deck + even adds the dials it used to lack. **Want the best of both → run a small button box for the blind-find controls and a Stream Deck for everything else.** And if your wheel still has empty buttons, the right answer this month is **neither** — finish [the rest of your rig](/racing/configurator) first and come back when you've genuinely run out of inputs.
