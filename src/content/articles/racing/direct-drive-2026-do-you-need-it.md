The first time a real direct-drive base loads up under your hands, something rewires. The wheel stops being a controller and becomes a sensor. You feel the front tires bite, then the exact moment they let go — not a vibration pretending to be grip, but the grip itself, transmitted straight from a motor to your palms with no belt, no gears, nothing in between.

That is the whole pitch for direct drive. And in 2026, the pitch has finally outrun its price tag.

## What direct drive actually changed

Belt and gear force feedback worked the way you would build a cheap printer. A motor spins, a belt or a set of plastic gears multiplies and redirects that motion, and somewhere down the chain a steering wheel moves. Every link in that chain adds lag, backlash, and noise. The wheel tells you something *happened* a few milliseconds after it did, smeared through rubber and plastic.

A direct-drive base bolts the wheel straight onto the motor shaft. The motor is the steering column. When the simulation says the front axle just hit a kerb, the torque arrives now, at full resolution, with the texture intact. That is the entire difference, and it is not subtle.

Here is the part nobody selling you a wheel will lead with: **the jump from belt to direct drive matters far more than the jump from one DD base to a stronger one.** Going from a Logitech G29 to a 5Nm base is a revelation. Going from 5Nm to 12Nm is a refinement. Going from 12Nm to 21Nm is a rounding error for most of us.

## How much torque do you actually need?

This is the question the whole bay exists to answer honestly, so let me give you the number first and the reasoning after. **For 99% of drivers, 9-15Nm is the honest sweet spot, and even 5Nm is genuinely enough to be competitive.** Anything above 15Nm is buying realism in heavy cars, not lap time.

Torque does three jobs. It sets the *ceiling* on how hard the wheel can push back. It influences how much fine *detail* you can feel underneath the big forces. And it sets how *fatiguing* a long stint gets. The trap is assuming more is always better on all three. It is not — detail is more about the motor and encoder quality than raw Nm, and fatigue gets *worse* as you climb.

| **3.9-5Nm** | Crisp, light, full DD detail at a modest ceiling | First DD, GT/formula casual, console | **MOZA R3** (3.9Nm), **Cammus C5** (5Nm) |

Read that table once and the marketing arms race dissolves. The reason brands sell 21Nm and 28Nm flagships is not that you need them — it is that "more Nm" is the easiest spec to print on a box.

## The diminishing-returns wall

I keep a simple rule when someone asks me to spec their first real base: **buy the torque you will use at 70%, not 100%.** Almost nobody runs a base at its maximum — you would be arm-wrestling the car all race. A 12Nm base set to a comfortable 8Nm in software gives you a strong, honest signal with headroom in reserve. A 21Nm base set to that same 8Nm gives you... the same 8Nm, minus several hundred dollars and a louder, hotter, heavier box on your desk.

The detail you feel — the texture of the asphalt, the tiny corrections as the rear steps out — comes from the motor's smoothness and the encoder's resolution, not its peak force. This is why **Simagic's Alpha Evo** line earned its reputation: its low-inertia servos and 21-bit encoders feel more *detailed* than rivals at the same Nm, which is the spec that actually matters once you are past 8Nm or so.

## Who should skip direct drive

Honest-broker moment. **Direct drive is not for everyone, and a 12Nm base is a waste for some buyers.** Skip it, or stay entry-level, if:

- You play a few hours a month and just want to mess around. A belt wheel is fine and you will not notice what you are missing.
- You have a desk-clamp-only setup and no appetite for a stand. Real DD torque will flex or tear a flimsy desk mount — and that is a [rig decision](/racing/configurator), not a base decision.
- You are buying purely on torque numbers to win an internet argument. You will spend triple for feel you cannot use.

For everyone else — anyone who races leagues, chases consistency, or just wants the car to talk back — direct drive is the single biggest upgrade in the hobby, and it is now cheaper than a mid-tier belt wheel was three years ago.

## The verdict

In 2026 the question is no longer *whether* to go direct drive — belt and gear wheels have collapsed into sub-$200 toy territory and the entry to real DD starts under $300. The question is *how much torque*, and the honest answer is less than the box wants you to buy. Start at 5-8Nm if money is tight, land at 9-15Nm if you can, and treat anything above that as a luxury you are choosing, not a need you are filling. Spend the torque you saved on pedals and a rig that does not flex. That is the setup you will not be itching to replace in twelve months.
