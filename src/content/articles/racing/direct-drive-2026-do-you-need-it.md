Verdict first: **in 2026, yes — buy direct drive, and buy less torque than the box wants you to.** The question stopped being *whether* to go DD the moment entry bases fell into the same $350-500 band as a good belt wheel. Belt and gear force feedback can't touch a direct-drive motor for clarity, and the price gap that used to justify starting on belt has closed. The real question is *how much torque*, and the honest answer is that **5Nm is enough to start and 9-15Nm is the sweet spot most drivers never outgrow.** Spend the money you save on pedals and a rig that doesn't flex.

The first time a real direct-drive base loads up under your hands, something rewires. The wheel stops being a controller and becomes a sensor. You feel the front tires bite, then the exact moment they let go — not a vibration pretending to be grip, but the grip itself, transmitted straight from a motor to your palms with no belt, no gears, nothing in between. That is the whole pitch for direct drive. And in 2026, the pitch has finally outrun its price tag.

## What direct drive actually changed

Belt and gear force feedback worked the way you'd build a cheap printer. A motor spins, a belt or a set of plastic gears multiplies and redirects that motion, and somewhere down the chain a steering wheel moves. Every link in that chain adds lag, backlash, and noise. The wheel tells you something *happened* a few milliseconds after it did, smeared through rubber and plastic.

A direct-drive base bolts the wheel straight onto the motor shaft. The motor *is* the steering column. When the simulation says the front axle just hit a kerb, the torque arrives now, at full resolution, with the texture intact. The gap isn't subtle, and the spec sheets bear it out: **gear-driven wheels top out around 2-3Nm with a mechanical, notchy feel; belt wheels manage 4-6Nm, smoother but softened; direct drive spans 6-30+Nm with the sharpest, fastest force changes on the market.**

Here's the part nobody selling you a wheel will lead with: **the jump from belt to direct drive matters far more than the jump from one DD base to a stronger one.** Going from a Logitech G29 to a 5Nm base is a revelation. Going from 5Nm to 12Nm is a refinement. Going from 12Nm to 21Nm is a rounding error for most of us.

## The 2026 change: the price argument is gone

For years the honest advice was "start on belt, it's cheaper." That advice is dead. Entry direct drive now starts around $300 and lands squarely in the same $350-500 band as a quality belt wheel like the Thrustmaster T300. When a categorically better technology costs the same as the thing it replaces, the decision makes itself.

The floor is genuinely low now. The **Cammus C5** hides its 5Nm motor inside the wheel itself and sells as a complete bundle — base, wheel, pedals, clamp — for around $299-328. The **MOZA R3** Xbox bundle brings real direct drive to consoles for roughly $319-399 on sale, wheel and pedals included. On PlayStation, the **Thrustmaster T598** puts a 5Nm axial DD motor, wheel and pedals on your desk in one box. None of these is a torque monster — and none needs to be to make the point.

## How much torque do you actually need?

This is the question the whole bay exists to answer honestly, so here's the number first and the reasoning after. **For 99% of drivers, 9-15Nm is the sweet spot, and even 5Nm is genuinely enough to be competitive.** Reviewers and the community land in the same place: 3-8Nm covers casual and entry racers, 10-18Nm is the serious band, and everything above 15Nm is buying realism in heavy cars, not lap time.

Torque does three jobs. It sets the *ceiling* on how hard the wheel can push back. It influences how much fine *detail* you can feel underneath the big forces. And it sets how *fatiguing* a long stint gets. The trap is assuming more is always better on all three. It isn't — detail is more about the motor and encoder quality than raw Nm, and fatigue gets *worse* as you climb.

|--------|------|---------------|---------------|
| **3.9-5Nm** | Crisp, light, full DD detail at a modest ceiling | First DD, GT/formula casual, console | **MOZA R3** (3.9Nm), **Cammus C5** (5Nm), **Thrustmaster T598** (5Nm) |
| **5.5-9Nm** | Confident, plenty of bite, no obvious GT ceiling | Most beginners and intermediates | **MOZA R5** (5.5Nm), **Fanatec CSL DD** (5-8Nm), **MOZA R9** (9Nm) |
| **9-15Nm** | Strong, immersive, real headroom for heavy cars | The enthusiast endgame for most | **MOZA R12** (12Nm), **Simagic Alpha Evo** (12Nm), **Fanatec ClubSport DD** (15Nm firmware) |
| **16-18Nm** | Maximum formula realism, peak detail at the limit | Hardcore racers, formula specialists | **Simucube 2 Sport** (17Nm), **Asetek Forte** (18Nm), **Simagic Alpha Evo Pro** (18Nm) |
| **21-28Nm** | Beyond what most can hold for a full race | Pros, masochists, bragging rights | **MOZA R21** (21Nm), **Asetek Invicta** (27Nm), **Simagic Evo Ultra** (28Nm) |

Read that table once and the marketing arms race dissolves. The reason brands sell 21Nm and 28Nm flagships isn't that you need them — it's that "more Nm" is the easiest spec to print on a box.

## The diminishing-returns wall

I keep a simple rule when someone asks me to spec their first real base: **buy the torque you'll use at 70%, not 100%.** Almost nobody runs a base at its maximum — you'd be arm-wrestling the car all race. A 12Nm base set to a comfortable 8Nm in software gives you a strong, honest signal with headroom in reserve. A 21Nm base set to that same 8Nm gives you... the same 8Nm, minus several hundred dollars and a louder, hotter, heavier box on your desk.

The detail you feel — the texture of the asphalt, the tiny corrections as the rear steps out — comes from the motor's smoothness and the encoder's resolution, not its peak force. This is why **Simagic's Alpha Evo** line earned its reputation: its low-inertia servos and 21-bit encoders feel more *detailed* than rivals at the same Nm, which is the spec that actually matters once you're past 8Nm or so. Its 18Nm Alpha Evo Pro puts frontier torque on your desk for around $699 — but you'll run it well below max, which is exactly the point.

## The frontier: when smoothness becomes the point

Past the sweet spot, you're not buying grip — you're buying slew rate, encoder resolution and cogging-free feel. The **Simucube 2 Sport** (17Nm) is the smoothness reference: a machined-metal body, 22-bit absolute encoder and the near-immortal SQR quick release, with zero cogging or torque ripple. The **Asetek Forte** (18Nm) brings industrial build and a 22-bit encoder whose feel reviewers struggle to tell apart from Simucube's flagship, at a fraction of the cost. Neither will make you faster than a 12Nm base — they'll make the drive feel more like the real thing, which is a different purchase entirely.

## The setup tax nobody quotes you

- **Mounting.** Anything 12Nm and up needs a 4-bolt mount and a rigid wheel stand or 8020 rig. Real DD torque will flex or tear a flimsy desk clamp — that's a [rig decision](/racing/configurator), not a base decision. Budget the mount when you budget the base.
- **Power supply tiers.** Several "entry" bases (Fanatec CSL DD, GT DD Pro) only hit their higher torque figure with an optional Boost Kit 180. Confirm which supply is in the box before you assume 8Nm.
- **Firmware changes the spec.** A May 2026 Fanatec firmware update raised the ClubSport DD to 15Nm for free. A base's real number can differ from the box — check current firmware notes.
- **Ecosystem lock-in.** Fanatec's QR2 quick release can disable force feedback on non-Fanatec rims. If you already own third-party wheels, factor that in before buying into any ecosystem.

## Who should skip direct drive

Honest-broker moment. **Direct drive isn't for everyone, and a 12Nm base is a waste for some buyers.** Stay on belt, or stay entry-level, if:

- You play a few hours a month and just want to mess around on a sub-$300 budget. A belt wheel is fine and you won't notice what you're missing.
- You have a desk-clamp-only setup and no appetite for a stand. Real DD torque will flex or tear a flimsy desk mount.
- You're buying purely on torque numbers to win an internet argument. You'll spend triple for feel you can't use — the money is better spent on pedals, which is the entire argument in [Pedals Over Power](/racing/pedals-over-power).

For everyone else — anyone who races leagues, chases consistency, or just wants the car to talk back — direct drive is the single biggest upgrade in the hobby, and at $400 it's now cheaper than a mid-tier belt wheel was three years ago.

## The buy order

1. Decide belt vs DD by budget: sub-$300 casual, belt is fine; $400+, go direct drive.
2. Pick your platform first — PS5 narrows you to Fanatec/Thrustmaster/Logitech immediately.
3. Choose torque by car type, not the biggest number: 5-9Nm to start, 9-12Nm for most, 15Nm+ only for heavy/no-assist cars.
4. Budget the 4-bolt mount and a rigid stand in the same cart as the base.
5. Spend leftover money on pedals (a load-cell brake) before more torque.
6. Check current firmware and which power supply ships in the box.
7. Map the whole rig in the [racing configurator](/racing/configurator) before you check out.

## The verdict

In 2026 the question is no longer *whether* to go direct drive — belt and gear wheels have collapsed into sub-$300 toy territory and the entry to real DD starts around $300, in the same band as a good belt wheel. The question is *how much torque*, and the honest answer is less than the box wants you to buy. Start at 5-8Nm if money is tight, land at 9-15Nm if you can, and treat anything above that as a luxury you're choosing, not a need you're filling. Spend the torque you saved on pedals and a rig that doesn't flex. That's the setup you won't be itching to replace in twelve months.
