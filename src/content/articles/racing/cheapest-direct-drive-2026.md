Verdict first: **the cheapest genuine direct drive in 2026 is the [Cammus C5](/racing/gear/cammus-c5) (~$249-299, 5Nm) if you just want real DD feel for the least money, or the [MOZA R3](/racing/gear/moza-r3-xbox-bundle) bundle (~$319, 3.9Nm) if you're on Xbox or want an upgrade ladder waiting.** But the single smartest budget move isn't the floor at all: it's stretching one rung to the [MOZA R12](/racing/gear/moza-r12) (~$429, 12Nm), the buy-once-and-stop base most drivers never outgrow. On PS5, the [Fanatec GT DD Pro](/racing/gear/fanatec-gt-dd-pro) or [Thrustmaster T598](/racing/gear/thrustmaster-t598) are the entry answers.

For years, "direct drive" and "affordable" did not belong in the same sentence. Real DD meant a four-figure base before you added a wheel, pedals, or a stand strong enough to hold it. The entry tier was belt wheels, the rubber-band stuff, and that was that. That wall fell in 2025, and in 2026 the rubble is gorgeous: you can now buy a complete, genuine motor-on-the-shaft direct-drive kit, base plus wheel plus pedals, for under $320. The headline truth is that **the floor is now so good that the hard question isn't "can I afford real DD?" — it's "should I spend $100 more and never shop for a base again?"**

The **[Cammus C5](/racing/gear/cammus-c5)** is the literal cheapest way in — often under $300 for the wheel with its motor built into the back, no separate base. It's the purest "I want real direct drive for the least money" answer, and 5Nm holding (peaking near 7Nm) is genuinely enough to feel the road. The catch: Cammus is a budget specialist, not an upgrade ecosystem. You're buying a great value box, not a ladder to climb, and the ownership polish has historically trailed the spec sheet.

The **[MOZA R3](/racing/gear/moza-r3-xbox-bundle)** trades a little torque (3.9Nm) for two things the Cammus can't offer: it's officially licensed for **Xbox**, and it's the bottom rung of MOZA's deep ladder. Outgrow it and you slot into the R5, R9, R12, all the way to the R21, keeping much of your peripheral investment. For console players leaving Logitech G920/G923 territory, or anyone who suspects they'll catch the bug, the R3 is the smarter long game even at slightly less torque.

## The value sweet spot: MOZA R12

Here's the number that reframes the whole budget: **the [MOZA R12](/racing/gear/moza-r12) is $429.** That's roughly $110 above the cheapest MOZA bundle and about $130 above the Cammus C5, and for it you jump from 3.9-5Nm to a full **12Nm** on MOZA's NexGen 4.0 algorithm with a 21-bit encoder. Twelve newton-metres is the torque ceiling the overwhelming majority of drivers never push past, which means the R12 isn't a stepping stone, it's a destination. The V2 refresh added the 21-bit encoder and better thermals without raising the price.

The deeper reason it wins the budget conversation is the ladder behind it. Buy an R12 and you've bought into a system, R5, [R9](/racing/gear/moza-r9), R16, R21, that shares quick releases, wheels and the excellent Pit House software. So even the "buy the floor" crowd should pause here: for the price of a nicer starter bundle, the R12 is the base you stop shopping for. The catch is real, though: it's PC-only and 12Nm needs a proper 4-bolt mount, not a desk clamp.

## The PlayStation floor: GT DD Pro and T598

Console buyers don't get the open field PC racers do. If you're on **PS5**, the cheapest paths into real direct drive are two bundles. The [Fanatec GT DD Pro](/racing/gear/fanatec-gt-dd-pro) is the established answer, officially licensed for Gran Turismo and sold as a complete kit with wheel, pedals and clamp. It runs 5Nm in the base bundle ($700) and 8Nm with the included Boost Kit 180 in the step-up bundle ($850). Eight newton-metres is modest on paper, but at this tier the PS5 license and the deep Fanatec ecosystem matter more than the torque number. Fanatec runs frequent sales, so check the current bundle price before you commit.

The value-minded PlayStation alternative is the [Thrustmaster T598](/racing/gear/thrustmaster-t598) (~$400-500 bundle), a Direct Axial Drive base with 5Nm constant torque that ships with a detachable rim and Raceline LTE pedals for PS5, PS4 and PC. It undercuts the GT DD Pro and is a genuinely credible entry, though its bundled rim is the first thing enthusiasts tend to outgrow.

## The Fanatec wildcard: CSL DD

The [Fanatec CSL DD](/racing/gear/fanatec-csl-dd) is the odd one out on this list because it's a base, not a bundle. At around $350 for the base alone you'll spend more by the time you add a wheel and pedals (the Ready2Race bundles start near $600), but you get 5Nm on the stock supply and **8Nm with the optional Boost Kit 180**, plus native **Xbox** support and entry into the most mature accessory catalog in the hobby. It's the pick if your real plan is a Fanatec ecosystem and you just want to start at the bottom of it. Just budget honestly: a complete CSL DD setup costs more than the cheapest MOZA or Cammus bundle, and the QR2 quick release disables force feedback on non-Fanatec rims unless you add a Podium Hub, the classic lock-in tax. (We don't have a verified in-house photo of the CSL DD base yet, so it's text-only here; the Amazon listing has current images.)

## What you actually give up at the bottom

Let me be the honest broker, because the marketing won't be. Going this cheap costs you real things, just not the things you'd fear.

**You do NOT give up the core experience.** The force feedback is genuine direct drive. The detail, the immediacy, the way the wheel goes light over a crest, all of it is there. A 3.9Nm or 5Nm DD base out-feels a $400 belt wheel comprehensively. This is the whole point: the floor is now extraordinary.

**You DO give up torque ceiling.** As covered in [Direct Drive in 2026](/racing/direct-drive-2026-do-you-need-it), 5Nm is plenty for GT and formula racing and most people never use more, but you'll feel the ceiling in heavy cars and big kerb strikes. It's a real limit, just one most drivers happily live under, and it's the single biggest argument for stretching to the R12.

**You DO give up pedal quality.** This is the one that actually matters. Bundle pedals at this price are basic two-pedal, potentiometer-based sets, they sense travel, not pressure. That makes consistent braking harder, and braking consistency is where lap time lives. The first upgrade most people should make isn't a stronger base but a load-cell pedal set, the full argument in [Pedals Over Power](/racing/pedals-over-power).

**You sometimes give up the rim.** Bundled wheels are functional plastic GT rims. Fine to start. Just know that the moment you want a nicer rim, the quick release on your base decides which rims you can even use, and the integrated Cammus C5 can't swap rims at all.

## The setup tax nobody quotes you

- **Boost Kits are not always in the box.** The Fanatec CSL DD and GT DD Pro only hit their higher 8Nm figure with the optional (or bundle-included) Boost Kit 180. Confirm which power supply ships before you assume you're getting 8Nm.
- **12Nm needs a real mount.** If you stretch to the R12, note it uses a 4-bolt mount and will overpower a cheap desk clamp. Budget a rigid stand in the same cart. The 3.9-5Nm bundles are fine on a sturdy desk clamp; the sweet-spot base is not.
- **PSU and cooling matter at the bottom too.** Integrated designs like the Cammus C5 run active cooling fans; the tiny bundle bases can heat-soak and reduce torque in long stints. It's rarely a dealbreaker, but it's real.
- **Shallow upgrade paths cost you later.** Cammus and Thrustmaster's entry paths are thinner than MOZA's or Fanatec's ladders. If you buy the cheapest thing and then get serious, you may replace the whole kit rather than just the base, the exact resale cycle the R12 stretch avoids.
- **Console lock is a spec, not a suggestion.** The Cammus C5, MOZA R5/R9/R12 are PC-only. Buying one for a console you can't return it against is the most expensive mistake at this tier.

## The buy order

1. Pick your platform first, PS5 narrows you to Fanatec/Thrustmaster immediately, Xbox to the MOZA R3 or Fanatec CSL DD.
2. Decide honestly whether this is a toe in the water or a hobby you already know you'll commit to. That answer, not the price, chooses your rung.
3. If committed and on PC, stretch to the [MOZA R12](/racing/gear/moza-r12) and stop shopping for a base. If truly budget-fixed, buy the [Cammus C5](/racing/gear/cammus-c5) or [MOZA R3](/racing/gear/moza-r3-xbox-bundle) with a clear conscience.
4. Earmark your next ~$150-250 for a load-cell brake before any torque upgrade.
5. Budget a rigid mount for anything 12Nm and up; a sturdy desk clamp is fine below that.
6. Confirm the Boost Kit / power supply and console license before you check out. Map the whole rig in the [racing configurator](/racing/configurator) first.

## Who should skip the value stretch

If you're genuinely **testing whether sim racing sticks**, buy the floor, a Cammus C5 or MOZA R3 teaches everything a 12Nm base does, minus the ceiling, and you can resell it cleanly if it's not for you. If you race **exclusively on console**, the R12 isn't even an option, so go straight to the GT DD Pro, T598 or CSL DD. And if you're a **casual GT cruiser** who races occasionally and doesn't chase lap times, the cheapest real DD is all you'll ever want. The R12 stretch is the right call for the *most* budget PC buyers who suspect they're hooked, not for every person.

## The verdict

If you want the cheapest real direct drive that exists, buy the **Cammus C5** at ~$249-299 and enjoy 5Nm of genuine DD feel for less than a belt wheel cost in 2022. If you race on **Xbox** or want a clear upgrade ladder, the **MOZA R3** bundle is worth the slightly lower torque for the ecosystem it opens. On **PS5**, start with the **GT DD Pro** or **Thrustmaster T598**. But if you're on PC and you already suspect you're hooked, the smartest budget decision isn't the floor at all, it's stretching about $100 to the **MOZA R12** and never shopping for a base again. Whatever rung you pick, put your next ~$200 into load-cell pedals before a stronger base. That's the upgrade that makes you faster.
