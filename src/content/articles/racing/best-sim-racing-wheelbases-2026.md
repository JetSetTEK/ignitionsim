The [Simagic Alpha Evo Pro](/racing/gear/simagic-evo-pro) puts 18Nm on your desk for ~$699.

The wheelbase is the single most consequential purchase in a sim racing rig. It's the part you feel every millisecond — the one component whose force feedback either tells you the truth about the car or lies to you. And it's the part the marketing machine works hardest to confuse you about, because torque is the easiest number to print on a box and the hardest spec to actually need. The headline truth nobody selling 21Nm wants to say: **most drivers are happiest between 9 and 15Nm, and a 12Nm base is a destination, not a stepping stone.**

## How much torque do you actually need?

Before the ranking, the reality check that saves the most money: **torque buys you headroom, not lap time.** Five newton-metres is genuinely enough to learn car control and feel the front axle load up. Twelve is the ceiling the overwhelming majority of drivers never push past. Everything above 15Nm is for heavy historic cars, no-power-steering trucks, and the specific pleasure of a wheel that can hurt you — not for going faster.

So spend the torque money once, in the sweet spot, and put the rest toward pedals and a mount that won't flex. That single decision is what separates a rig that feels great on the first Saturday from a 21Nm base bolted to a desk clamp it tears loose by lap three.

## Best overall: MOZA R12

The [MOZA R12](/racing/gear/moza-r12) (~$429, 12Nm, now shipping as the V2) is the base I'd put in most rigs without hesitation. It lands exactly in the torque sweet spot — enough to feel weight transfer, kerbs and the front axle loading up, with real headroom for heavier cars — inside a compact body and at a price that still feels almost unfair. The NexGen 4.0 algorithm and 21-bit encoder deliver clean, detailed feedback, and MOZA's Pit House software is the easiest tuning suite in the business.

But the deeper reason it wins is the ecosystem behind it. Buy an R12 and you've bought into a ladder — [R5](/racing/gear/moza-r5), [R9](/racing/gear/moza-r9), R16, R21 — that shares quick releases, wheels and software. It's a buy-once-and-stop base for the vast majority of racers, and a sane on-ramp for the few who'll climb higher. **Who it's for:** the PC enthusiast who wants to buy the right thing once. The catch: it's PC-only and needs a proper 4-bolt mount.

## Best value: Simagic Alpha Evo Pro

If "best overall" is about balance, **best value is about the steepest performance-per-dollar curve in the market** — and the [Simagic Alpha Evo Pro](/racing/gear/simagic-evo-pro) (~$699, 18Nm) owns it. Eighteen newton-metres for under $700 is a number comparable Asetek and Simucube bases simply can't touch, and the line's low-inertia servo keeps all that force feeling clean rather than gritty. The open QR-A quick release finally lets Simagic owners run third-party rims, breaking the brand's old lock-in.

Is 18Nm overkill? For most people, yes — you'll run it well below max. But the Pro's value is so aggressive that the headroom comes at almost no premium over a 12Nm base, which is what makes it the enthusiast's smart-money pick. The same Alpha EVO family also sells in 9Nm and 12Nm trims if you'd rather buy the sweet spot and pocket the difference.

## Best budget: the entry direct-drive tier

For the smallest credible entry into real direct drive, the [Fanatec CSL DD](/racing/gear/fanatec-csl-dd) (~$350, sold now as the CSL DD QR2) remains the benchmark years after launch. It runs 5Nm on the stock supply — plenty to learn on — and steps up to 8Nm with the optional Boost Kit 180. Crucially, it's one of the very few entry bases that runs on **Xbox** as well as PC, and it opens the door to Fanatec's enormous accessory catalog.

The pure-value alternative is the [Cammus C5](/racing/gear/cammus-c5) (~$299), the cheapest genuine direct-drive bundle on the market, with an integrated motor that saves desk space. It's a complete starter kit rather than a platform you build on — shallow upgrade path, PC-only — but for a budget-first newcomer who just wants real DD feel, nothing's cheaper.

## Best for PlayStation: Fanatec GT DD Pro

Console buyers don't get the open field PC racers do, and on PS5 the [Fanatec GT DD Pro](/racing/gear/fanatec-gt-dd-pro) (8Nm with the included Boost Kit 180) is the established answer. It's one of the only credible direct-drive options officially licensed for Gran Turismo, it ships as a complete bundle with wheel and pedals, and it plugs into the full Fanatec ecosystem. Eight newton-metres is modest on paper, but at this tier the PS5 license and bundle convenience matter more than the torque number. Fanatec runs frequent sales, so check the current bundle price before you commit.

The premium console pick is the [Logitech G PRO Racing Wheel](/racing/gear/logitech-g-pro) (~$999, 11Nm), which spans PS5 or Xbox (per SKU) plus PC, pairs TRUEFORCE feedback with superb build quality, and ships with the refined PRO rim. It's the nicest console base you can buy — you just pay a real premium for the torque on offer.

## The premium tier: when smoothness becomes the point

Past the sweet spot, you're paying for slew rate, encoder resolution, and cogging-free feel rather than raw grip. Three bases define the top:

The [Fanatec ClubSport DD+](/racing/gear/fanatec-clubsport-dd-plus) (~$899–$999) is the reference-class Fanatec base. It's rated at 15Nm, and the May 2026 firmware update pushed its holding torque to 18Nm for free — the same update that took the standard [ClubSport DD](/racing/gear/fanatec-clubsport-dd) ($699) to 15Nm. If you're committed to the Fanatec ecosystem on PC or Xbox, this is the base.

The [Simucube 2 Sport](/racing/gear/simucube-2-sport) (~$1,199, 17Nm) is the smoothness reference — zero-cogging feel and the near-immortal SQR quick release. For the fidelity purist over the torque chaser, this is the one your hands remember.

At the frontier, the [Asetek Forte](/racing/gear/asetek-forte) (~$1,199, 18Nm) and the flagship [Asetek Invicta](/racing/gear/asetek-invicta) (27Nm, ~€1,576 direct from Asetek) bring a 22-bit absolute encoder and a blistering 9.4 Nm/ms slew rate — the sharpest, fastest force changes on the market, with industrial build to match.

## The setup tax nobody quotes you

- **Mounting.** Anything 12Nm and up needs a 4-bolt mount and a rigid wheel stand or 8020 rig. A desk clamp on a big base is how you crack a desk and kill your feedback in the same afternoon. Budget the mount when you budget the base.
- **The Fanatec QR2 question.** Fanatec's ecosystem is deep, but the QR2 quick release can disable force feedback on non-Fanatec rims. If you already own third-party wheels, factor that in before buying into the ecosystem.
- **Firmware changes the spec.** The May 2026 Fanatec firmware raised the ClubSport DD to 15Nm and the DD+ to 18Nm holding torque for free. Always check current firmware notes — a base's real number can differ from the box.
- **Power supply tiers.** Several "entry" bases (CSL DD, GT DD Pro) only hit their higher torque figure with an optional Boost Kit 180. Confirm which supply is in the box.

## The buy order

1. Pick your platform first — PS5 narrows you to Fanatec/Logitech immediately.
2. Choose torque by car type, not by the biggest number: 9-12Nm for most, 15Nm+ only for heavy/no-assist cars.
3. Budget the 4-bolt mount and a rigid stand in the same cart as the base.
4. Spend leftover money on pedals (a load-cell brake) before more torque.
5. Check current firmware and which power supply ships in the box.
6. Map the whole rig in the [racing configurator](/racing/configurator) before you check out.

## Who should skip the top pick

If you race exclusively on **PlayStation**, the MOZA R12 isn't an option — go straight to the Fanatec GT DD Pro. If you're a genuine **first-timer on a tight budget**, a 5Nm Cammus C5 or CSL DD teaches everything and frees up money for pedals that matter more than most people admit. And if you're a **fidelity obsessive** with the budget, the R12's value is wasted on you — your hands will want the Simucube or Asetek smoothness. The R12 wins "best overall" because it's the right answer for the *most* people, not every person.
