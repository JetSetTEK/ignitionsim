Verdict first: **don't buy anything on day one — fly the gamepad you already own, then buy the one control that matches your airplane.** For general-aviation flyers that first real purchase is a combined yoke-and-throttle (the [Logitech G PRO Flight Yoke System](/flight/gear/logitech-g-pro-flight-yoke-system) at ~$180, or the [Honeycomb Alpha](/flight/gear/honeycomb-alpha-yoke) at ~$300 if you already know GA is sticking). For Airbus-leaning newcomers it's the ~$80 [Thrustmaster TCA Sidestick](/flight/gear/thrustmaster-tca-sidestick-airbus). For Xbox players it's a console all-in-one. Everything else — pedals, a second throttle, panels, head tracking — comes later, in that order.

Microsoft Flight Simulator 2024 did something the hobby hadn't seen in years: it made the front door wide. Hundreds of thousands of people loaded a Cessna, wobbled into the air on a gamepad, and immediately asked the same question — what do I actually buy first? The honest answer is a staircase, not a leap. Below are three concrete builds at roughly **$150, $300 and $500-600**. Spend at the tier that matches how serious you already know you are, buy one piece at a time, and let the cockpit grow under you.

## Start with the controller you already own

You do not need to spend a dollar to start flying. An Xbox controller or any gamepad works in MSFS 2024, and with the assistance options turned up you can fly a respectable circuit. This is not a throwaway phase — it's the cheapest, smartest week you'll spend on the hobby. Fly the gamepad for a week and it tells you, for free, whether you lean toward puttering around in **general aviation** (GA) piston aircraft or toward big tube-liners. That single decision shapes every purchase after it, because a yoke that makes a Cessna sing is the wrong control for an A320.

The gamepad's ceiling is real, though. Throttle on a trigger or a stick has no detents and no resolution, and pitch trim on a d-pad is misery. When precise approaches start feeling like a fight against the hardware rather than the airplane, it's time for the first real device — and which device depends entirely on the answer that week gave you.

## Build 1 — The ~$150 "get airborne properly" cockpit

The single best first upgrade for a **GA flyer on PC** is a combined yoke-and-throttle device. The [Logitech G PRO Flight Yoke System](/flight/gear/logitech-g-pro-flight-yoke-system) (**$179.99** at Logitech's official store as of July 2026) is the default pick: a stainless-steel-shaft, self-centering yoke plus a separate three-lever throttle quadrant in one box, with 75 programmable controls. It's plastic, the aging potentiometer axes can drift over years of use, and it won't impress a cockpit builder. None of that matters when you're learning. It gives you a proper pitch-and-roll yoke, real throttle resolution, and a fistful of buttons for trim and flaps — and it's the gateway into the stackable Saitek panel ecosystem if you keep going.

For **airliner-leaning newcomers**, the alternative is the [Thrustmaster TCA Sidestick Airbus Edition](/flight/gear/thrustmaster-tca-sidestick-airbus) (~$80), a 1:1 A320 side-stick with contactless magnetic sensors that never wear out, a twist rudder and a built-in thrust lever, so it flies solo. It's the best value in flight sim and the correct rung-one buy for the Fenix A320. For **Xbox players** — who can't use the PC-only Logitech yoke — the [Turtle Beach VelocityOne Flightstick](/flight/gear/turtle-beach-velocityone-flight-stick) (~$130) is the console all-in-one: an Xbox- and PC-licensed stick with an integrated twist rudder, an OLED status display and 27 programmable buttons.

At this tier you do not buy pedals. Every device here has a twist or rocker rudder axis, and it's plenty for early flying.

## Build 2 — The ~$300 "I'm committed to GA" cockpit

This is where most people who stick with the hobby end up within a few months. The anchor is the [Honeycomb Alpha Flight Controls Yoke](/flight/gear/honeycomb-alpha-yoke) (**$299.99** at Honeycomb's official store; often less on sale). It's a 180-degree steel-shaft yoke on dual ball bearings with proper self-centering feel and almost no deadzone, plus an integrated switch and ignition panel right on the base — master, alternator, avionics, lights, and a five-position magneto/ignition knob. That panel alone removes a dozen mouse-clicks from every flight. It's plastic-bodied at the price and the aileron axis runs a touch light, but as the de-facto GA standard with broad MSFS 2024 and X-Plane 12 support, it's the easy buy for anyone flying Cessnas.

Run the Alpha alongside the throttle quadrant you already own from Build 1, or a cheap standalone quadrant, and add rudder pedals when budget allows. Hold off on the premium throttle for now; the Alpha by itself transforms GA flying, and a month of it teaches you exactly what you want next.

## Build 3 — The ~$500-600 enthusiast standard

The **Honeycomb Alpha + Bravo pairing** is the most-recommended GA cockpit in the hobby, and for good reason. At official MSRP the two run about **$600 together** ($299.99 each), though the bundle frequently sells for less — check current before you buy. The [Bravo Throttle Quadrant](/flight/gear/honeycomb-bravo-throttle) ($299.99 on its own) brings six swappable levers — single-lever GA, dual and quad commercial — plus a genuine autopilot panel, a 14-light annunciator, a trim wheel and a gear lever. Together the Alpha and Bravo cover the vast majority of what a GA or light-twin pilot touches, in hardware, without diving into menus.

This is the build I point most newcomers toward if they already know flight sim is going to stick. It scales: the natural next step is the [Honeycomb Charlie](/flight/gear/honeycomb-charlie-pedals) rudder pedals (~$350) to complete the matched three-piece deck in aluminum. I walk through that staged path in detail in [the GA cockpit upgrade path](/flight/honeycomb-flight-deck-upgrade-path).

## When rudder pedals actually matter

The most common beginner overspend is buying pedals on day one. You do not need them to learn. The twist axis on your yoke or stick, plus MSFS 2024's auto-rudder, handles taxiing and gentle yaw fine for months.

The moment to buy pedals is specific: when you start flying crosswind landings, when you're taxiing a taildragger, or when the twist motion starts cross-coupling your roll inputs in the flare. That's your cue. When it comes, the [Logitech G Flight Rudder Pedals](/flight/gear/logitech-g-flight-rudder-pedals) (**$179.99** official) with real differential toe brakes are the standard budget answer, and the aluminum [Honeycomb Charlie](/flight/gear/honeycomb-charlie-pedals) (~$350) is the step up. I go deeper in [the best rudder pedals guide](/flight/best-flight-sim-rudder-pedals).

## The Boeing exception

If your week on the gamepad told you your future is a Boeing glass cockpit rather than a Cessna, the beginner ladder splits early — and buying a GA yoke first is the expensive mistake. Your rung-one buy is the [Thrustmaster TCA Yoke Pack Boeing Edition](/flight/gear/thrustmaster-tca-yoke-boeing) (~$500), a 1:1 787-inspired yoke whose PENDUL_R mechanism swings the column through realistic airliner kinematics rather than sliding flat, bundled with a throttle quadrant. It's heavy, well-built, uses magnetic sensors, and is officially licensed for Xbox as well as PC. It's a bigger first spend than the GA path, but for a committed Boeing flyer it's the right half of the funnel. ## Who should skip all of this

Be honest with yourself. If you've flown the gamepad for two evenings and already feel done, do not spend $300 on a yoke that will become a shelf ornament. There's no shame in flying on a controller forever — plenty of people do, and MSFS 2024's assistance modes are built for exactly that. Hardware rewards repetition. Buy it when you've proven to yourself that you keep coming back.

And don't buy the obvious top pick against your own airplane. Don't buy the Honeycomb Alpha if you mainly fly the A320 — the ~$80 TCA Sidestick will serve you better and cost a quarter as much. Don't buy pedals before your yoke. Don't spend on head tracking or panels before you own a decent primary control. Match the tool to the mission, and buy the ladder in order.

## The setup tax nobody quotes you

- **Desk mounting isn't optional for yokes.** The Logitech and Honeycomb yokes clamp to a solid desk edge — a flimsy desk or a thin lip will flex and creak under the pull. Budget a real desk edge, not just the yoke.
- **Twist rudder is a stopgap, not a solution.** It's fine to start on, but it cross-couples with roll on some sticks, which is exactly the cue to add pedals later. Don't fight it forever.
- **Console vs PC splits your options.** Xbox players can't use the PC-only Logitech yoke or pedals; your path runs through the VelocityOne, the Xbox-licensed TCA Yoke Pack, or (soon) the TCA Sky Yoke. Check platform before you fall in love with a device.
- **Calibration takes a session.** Every new device wants a few minutes in the sim's sensitivity and deadzone settings before it feels right. Budget the time, not just the money — see [control curves and calibration](/flight/flight-sim-control-curves-calibration).

## The verdict

For nearly every new MSFS 2024 pilot, the answer is a staircase, not a leap. Start free on the gamepad for a week and let it name your airplane. Then buy rung one, matched to that airplane: a ~$180 Logitech yoke or ~$300 Honeycomb Alpha for GA, the ~$80 TCA Sidestick for Airbus, the ~$130 VelocityOne on Xbox, or the ~$500 TCA Yoke Pack for a committed Boeing flyer. Graduate to the Alpha + Bravo pairing once you know flight sim has its hooks in you, and add pedals only when a specific maneuver demands them. If you want help mapping a full build to your budget before you spend, run it through [the flight configurator](/flight/configurator) — then buy the next single piece, fly it until you've outgrown it, and let the cockpit grow under you.
