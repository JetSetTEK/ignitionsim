Verdict first: **buy the Honeycomb Alpha yoke, fly it for a few weeks, then add the Bravo throttle, and finish with rudder pedals last** — but in 2026 that last rung comes with an asterisk. The matching [Honeycomb Charlie pedals](/flight/gear/honeycomb-charlie-pedals) are, as of this update, hard to actually buy: the official store lists them at $349.99 but shows them **Out of Stock**, and their release has been stuck for years. The ladder is still right. The third rung just often ends up being a different brand's pedals, and that's fine.

Nobody should buy a complete flight deck in one click. Even with the budget, dropping $600-plus of hardware on the desk at once teaches you nothing about what each piece does for you — and you'll have positioned all of it wrong, because you had no flying hours to tell you what feels right. The smarter move is staged: three pieces, each useful on its own, that add up to a complete general-aviation cockpit. Here's the order, what each stage changes about how you fly, and the honest word on where the Honeycomb ecosystem gets bumpy.

## The staged path at a glance

Before any single product, the reality check that saves the most money: **buy in the order your hands need the hardware, not in the order the marketing excites you.** Pitch and roll first, power second, rudder last.

| Stage | Piece | ~Price (official) | What it unlocks | When to buy |
|-------|-------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| 1 | [Alpha yoke](/flight/gear/honeycomb-alpha-yoke) | $299.99 | Pitch/roll, start-up switches | First — the foundation |
| 2 | [Bravo throttle](/flight/gear/honeycomb-bravo-throttle) | $299.99 | Power management, autopilot panel | When clicking the throttle/AP gets old |
| 3 | [Charlie pedals](/flight/gear/honeycomb-charlie-pedals) | $349.99 · **often out of stock** | Yaw, independent toe brakes | When crosswinds and taxiing demand it — buy whatever's in stock |

The two-piece Alpha + Bravo deck lands near $600 at list (Aircraft Spruce lists the bundle around $632; sale bundles occasionally dip under $500 — check current). Stage three is where the plan meets reality, so let's take the rungs in order.

## Stage 1 — The Alpha yoke (the foundation)

Start with the [Honeycomb Alpha Flight Controls](/flight/gear/honeycomb-alpha-yoke) (~$299.99 official; often less on sale). This is non-negotiable as the first piece, because pitch and roll are the controls you touch every single second you're in the air. Everything else is in service of the yoke.

The Alpha is a 180-degree steel-shaft yoke with genuine self-centering feel — not a mushy spring, but a defined return you can trim against. The detail that earns its keep is the integrated switch panel on the base: master, alternator and avionics switches, a lights row, and a five-position magneto and ignition knob. From the moment you install it, your engine start and shutdown flows move off the mouse and onto real switches. It's plastic-bodied at the price and the aileron axis runs a touch light next to the firm elevator, but as the de-facto GA standard with broad MSFS 2024 and X-Plane 12 support, it's the easy first buy for anyone flying Cessnas.

What stage 1 unlocks: precise hand-flying and a real start-up procedure. Fly the Alpha for a few weeks with the throttle you already own, and you'll quickly feel exactly what's missing — that gap is stage 2.

## Stage 2 — The Bravo throttle (the multiplier)

The [Honeycomb Bravo Throttle Quadrant](/flight/gear/honeycomb-bravo-throttle) (~$299.99 official) is where a single-control setup becomes a cockpit. It carries six swappable levers, so the same base reconfigures from a single-lever GA piston to a twin's throttle/prop/mixture layout to a quad-engine commercial setup. Underneath sits a trim wheel, a gear lever and a 14-light annunciator.

But the reason the Bravo is the most-recommended throttle in the hobby is the autopilot panel built into the top. Heading, altitude, vertical speed, course, the autopilot master and the mode buttons all live in physical hardware. The first time you set up a cruise and manage it entirely from real knobs instead of clicking a tiny on-screen panel, the Bravo justifies itself. Complex airliners like the Fenix A320 or PMDG 737 may need custom binding, and the default lever tension feels light to some pilots, but for GA and light twins it's a power-management cockpit in one box.

What stage 2 unlocks: real power management on multi-engine aircraft and hands-on autopilot. At this point you have a cockpit that handles the overwhelming majority of GA and light-twin flying without the mouse. If you're buying stages 1 and 2 together anyway, the **Alpha + Bravo bundle** is the standard one-buy GA cockpit — I compare it head-to-head with the airliner-focused alternative in [Honeycomb vs Thrustmaster TCA](/flight/honeycomb-vs-thrustmaster-tca).

## Stage 3 — Rudder pedals (the finisher), and the Charlie problem

Here's where the Honeycomb story gets honest. The matching [Honeycomb Charlie](/flight/gear/honeycomb-charlie-pedals) rudder pedals were designed to complete the deck in metal — an aluminum frame, belt-drive rudder, Hall-effect sensors that resist the drift that wears out cheaper potentiometer pedals, variable-pressure toe brakes and adjustable foot angle. On paper it's the ideal third rung. In practice, in 2026, it is very hard to actually buy.

Whatever set you buy, rudder pedals come last on purpose. The twist or rocker rudder on your earlier hardware carries you through your first months of flying; you add real pedals when yaw becomes the limiting factor — crosswind landings, taxiing taildraggers, coordinating turns. By then you have the hours to know you want them, and the good news is the pedal market has plenty of in-stock options that hit the same goals the Charlie was aiming for.

## When you outgrow the GA deck: the airliner detour

The Honeycomb ladder is a GA instrument, full stop. If your flying drifts toward airliners, the honest move is a parallel deck, not a bolt-on. Two rungs worth knowing:

- **Airbus** — the [Thrustmaster TCA Sidestick](/flight/gear/thrustmaster-tca-sidestick-airbus) (~$80) is the cheapest authentic A320 side-stick, and the [TCA Quadrant](/flight/gear/thrustmaster-tca-quadrant-airbus) (~$100) adds the Idle/Climb/Flex/TO-GA detents. Under $200 gets you a credible Fenix A320 cockpit before you spend a cent on panels.
- **Boeing** — the [TCA Yoke Pack](/flight/gear/thrustmaster-tca-yoke-boeing) (~$500) is built around a 1:1 787-inspired yoke whose PENDUL_R mechanism swings the column through realistic airliner kinematics, and it's Xbox-licensed.

## A note on mounting

The Honeycomb pieces are separate units, not a single rail system. The Alpha and Bravo clamp to the desk; the pedals sit on the floor. That works on a sturdy desk, but once all three are in place most people want consistent, repeatable positioning — that's the moment a desk mount or a basic cockpit rig starts to make sense, especially to stop the yoke walking around under hard control inputs. If you're stacking panels on top of the deck later, budget a powered USB hub (~$60) before you pass two panels; a full yoke-throttle-pedals-plus-panels stack out-draws your PC's own ports and drops devices mid-flight.

## Who should skip this ecosystem

If you fly airliners, skip the Honeycomb path entirely and build the parallel Airbus or Boeing deck above. And if you only ever fly the occasional Cessna circuit for fun, you don't need three pieces — the Alpha alone, or the Alpha plus a cheap quadrant, is a perfectly happy stopping point. There's no rule that says you must finish the set, and given Charlie's stock, plenty of pilots stop at two Honeycomb pieces plus a third-party pedal by choice.

## The verdict

The Honeycomb flight deck is the GA standard precisely because it rewards patience. Buy the **Alpha** first (~$300, in stock) and learn to hand-fly on it. Add the **Bravo** (~$300, in stock) when clicking the autopilot starts to grate, and let its built-in panel transform your cruise. Then add rudder pedals when yaw becomes the thing holding you back — reaching for the **Charlie** only if you find it genuinely in stock at a fair price, and otherwise grabbing an in-stock Logitech, VKB or VelocityOne set without a second thought. Each stage is useful the day it arrives, and the two-piece Honeycomb core lands near $600 built up in the order that makes you a better pilot at every step.
