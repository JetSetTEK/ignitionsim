MSFS 2024 did something its predecessor never properly did: it made helicopters a first-class part of the sim, with a real flight model that punishes you for sloppy hands and rewards a settled hover. And the first thing every fixed-wing simmer learns is that their gorgeous [Honeycomb yoke](/flight/gear/honeycomb-alpha-yoke) is *useless* here. A helicopter has no yoke. It has a cyclic, a collective, and pedals — three controls you work simultaneously, all the time, in a constant balancing act.

So before you spend a dime, understand the three controls and how sim hardware maps to them. Then I will give you the honest ladder from a repurposed HOTAS to a dedicated rig.

## The three controls, and what each does

- **Cyclic** — the stick between your knees. It tilts the rotor disc, moving the helicopter forward, back, left, right. In sim terms it is your **pitch and roll axes**, exactly like a joystick. The key requirement: a precise, low-drift center, because hover is a game of tiny corrections.
- **Collective** — the lever by your left hip you pull up to climb and push down to descend. It changes the pitch of all rotor blades at once, i.e. **total lift/power**. The key requirement: it must *stay where you leave it* — no self-centering spring. A throttle lever with friction is perfect; a self-centering throttle is not.
- **Anti-torque pedals** — the pedals that counter the tail rotor and point the nose. They are your **yaw axis**, and in a helicopter you are on them *constantly*, far more than in a fixed-wing aircraft.

That third point is the one that sinks beginners. Every power change with the collective twists the airframe, and the pedals are how you cancel it. You cannot fake this with a twist-rudder for long — your wrist gives out and the precision is not there.

### Make-do — fly helicopters with what you have

If you already own a center-sprung joystick and any throttle lever, you can fly helicopters tonight. Use the stick as the cyclic, assign a throttle lever to the collective, and limp along on a twist-rudder until you commit. It works well enough to learn whether you even like rotary flying before spending money. The twist-rudder is the weak link — it will frustrate you within an hour of hover practice.

### Budget proper — the setup most people should buy

The sweet spot is a **precise Hall-effect stick** as the cyclic, a throttle lever as the collective, and **dedicated rudder pedals**. The **Thrustmaster T.16000M** is the classic budget cyclic because its Hall-effect sensors hold center without drift — and a settled hover lives or dies on that center. Add a throttle quadrant lever for the collective and a set of [rudder pedals](/flight/best-flight-sim-rudder-pedals) and you are at roughly **$300-400** for a setup that flies the R44, the H125, and the Bell 407 properly. This is what I recommend to anyone who is serious but not all-in.

### HOTAS combo — one box, two jobs

A **Logitech G X56** (around $200-250) is a popular helicopter pick because it is a HOTAS whose throttle *detaches and repositions*, so you can sit it down on your left where a real collective lives. Configure the left throttle as the collective with friction so it stays parked, use the stick as the cyclic, and you have a tidy two-piece setup. You still need pedals — the X56 has a twist-rudder but you will outgrow it fast. For the binding and curve work this needs, the [control curves guide](/flight/flight-sim-control-curves-calibration) is worth a read; helicopters are far more sensitive to a good curve than fixed-wing.

### Dedicated — replica cyclic and collective

If helicopters are your *main* sim, dedicated gear exists. Makers like **Komodo Simulations** build replica cyclic grips and collectives with authentic switchgear, a twist throttle on the collective head, and proper friction. Paired with **pendular pedals** like the Thrustmaster TPR (~$600), a full dedicated rig lands around **$1,200-1,800**. It is wonderful and completely unnecessary for anyone who flies helicopters occasionally. Buy this only when you have logged serious hover hours and the budget tier is genuinely limiting you.

## The bindings that actually work

Hardware is only half the battle — MSFS 2024's defaults will fight you. The three fixes that matter:

- **Collective axis, inverted.** Assign your throttle lever to the *collective* axis, not the throttle, and invert it so pulling the lever *up* increases collective pitch. The backwards collective is the single most common complaint, and this is the fix.
- **Cyclic on pitch and roll** with a sensible center deadzone — small, not zero — and a softened curve near center so micro-corrections in the hover do not over-control.
- **Pedals on yaw**, full travel, and get comfortable being on them constantly. If you are still on a twist-rudder, this is where you will feel its limits hardest.

Throttles default to idle-at-bottom; a collective adds power as it rises. Flip the invert checkbox and 90% of "my heli flies backwards" frustration disappears in one click.</p>

A collective sits low and to your left, so plan the physical layout before you buy — mapping the cyclic, collective and pedal positions in [the Rig Configurator](/racing/configurator) saves you from a cramped, unflyable seat.

Get these three right and a wobbly, unflyable helicopter usually becomes a controllable one in the same session.

## Who should skip this

Skip dedicated helicopter hardware if you fly **mostly fixed-wing** and just want to dabble — your existing stick, a throttle lever, and pedals cover it for the price of nothing extra. Skip the **Logitech X56** if you do not want a HOTAS for anything else; a plain Hall-effect stick plus a throttle quadrant is cheaper and just as capable as a cyclic-and-collective pairing. And skip the full **dedicated rig** entirely unless helicopters are your primary sim and you have already worn out the budget tier. Most simmers never need to leave the ~$300-400 setup.

## Verdict

For the vast majority of MSFS 2024 simmers who want to fly helicopters seriously, the budget-proper setup wins: a **Hall-effect stick** as the cyclic, a **throttle lever** as the collective, and **dedicated rudder pedals**, for roughly $300-400. It flies the entire MSFS 2024 helicopter roster correctly, and the pedals carry over to your fixed-wing flying too. Repurpose a HOTAS like the X56 if you want one tidy box, and reach for dedicated Komodo gear only when rotary flying is the whole point of your sim. Whatever you buy, do not cheap out on the pedals — in a helicopter, your feet never stop working.
