A yoke and throttle change how your hands fly. Head tracking changes how your *head* flies — and on a flat monitor, that is a bigger deal than most people expect. The first time you look into a turn to keep the runway in view, or lean forward to clear the nose on a taildragger, the sim stops feeling like a screen and starts feeling like a window. It is the single cheapest upgrade that buys you situational awareness, and you do not need a VR headset to get it.

The catch is that "head tracking" means three very different products at three very different prices. Let me lay them out honestly — what each one costs, where each one wins, and who should not bother.

## What head tracking actually does

A 2D monitor only shows you a fixed forward view. To look around a cockpit you normally have to nudge a hat switch or pan with the mouse — clumsy, and it pulls your hand off the controls. Head tracking maps your real head movement to the in-sim camera. Turn your head left and the view pans left. Lean in and you move closer to the panel. Done well it is **6DOF** — six degrees of freedom — tracking yaw, pitch, roll, plus the three lean axes.

What you want from it:

- **Low latency** so the view does not lag behind your head and induce queasiness.
- **Smooth, low-noise tracking** so the view does not jitter when you hold still.
- **A sensible curve** so small movements near center are gentle and big looks still reach the side window.
- **Easy recenter** so you can reset the neutral view without diving into menus.

That last one matters more than reviews admit. You will recenter constantly, so bind it to a stick button you can reach without looking.

### TrackIR 5 — the precision standard

The **TrackIR 5** has been the enthusiast default for over a decade, and it earns it. It is an infrared camera that watches a reflective clip on your headset or a reflector hat, and it is *fast* — when you snap your head to check six in a Spitfire, the view is there instantly with no perceptible lag. That responsiveness is exactly what the Tobii gives up.

The trade-off is that you have to wear something. The clip lives on your headphones; the hat is the alternative if you fly without a headset. Some people never notice it, others find the clip annoying. At around **$150** for the camera (the clip or TrackClip Pro adds a little), it is also the cheaper of the two name-brand options, which surprises people who assume the eye tracker is the budget pick. It is not.

### Tobii Eye Tracker 5 — the hands-free one

The **Tobii Eye Tracker 5** (around **$299-339**) is a camera bar that sits under your monitor and tracks your eyes and face directly — nothing to wear, nothing to clip. It is **native to MSFS 2024**, so you plug it in, enable it in the sim, and you are flying. For a quick GA hop that zero-friction start is genuinely lovely; you do not think about it at all.

The honest downside is that it is a touch less responsive and a little noisier than TrackIR on fast movements. For normal GA or airliner flights that is a non-issue — it works great. For fast, aggressive head movement in a dogfight, you will feel TrackIR's edge. A common tip from owners: turn the *eye* tracking sensitivity way down and lean on the *head* tracking, which cuts the noise and feels more natural. If you mostly fly tube-liners and Cessnas and you hate wearing gear, the Tobii is the better experience despite the higher price.

### OpenTrack — the free 6DOF route

If you want head tracking for almost nothing, **OpenTrack** is the answer. It is free, open-source, actively maintained, and it accepts input from a plain webcam (face tracking via a companion like AITrack) or, for better results, an IR clip you build yourself paired with a modified PS3 Eye camera. Total cost can be **under $30** if you DIY the clip.

It is the most work — you configure the input source, the mapping curves, and the output protocol yourself — and it is less polished than the commercial options. But the tracking itself is real 6DOF and perfectly usable once dialed in. This is the route for tinkerers and tight budgets. If your time is worth more than the price gap, buy a TrackIR instead.

## Where head tracking sits next to VR

People constantly frame this as head tracking *versus* VR. It is not really a versus — they solve different problems, which I dig into in the [VR vs monitor breakdown](/flight/vr-vs-monitor-flight-sim). VR gives you true stereoscopic depth and a 1:1 cockpit you can lean into, at the cost of a headset on your face, a heavier GPU load, and harder-to-read small gauges. Head tracking keeps you on a flat monitor at full resolution with no weight and no extra GPU hit — it just adds look-around freedom. If you have already invested in a triple-monitor or single ultrawide setup and want it to feel alive, head tracking is the upgrade. If you want immersion above all, VR is the bigger jump.

## Pro tips before you buy

- **Bind recenter to a reachable button.** On your [yoke](/flight/gear/honeycomb-alpha-yoke) or stick, not deep in a menu. You will use it every flight.
- **Tune the curve, not just the sensitivity.** A gentle center and a steeper outer curve lets you read instruments without your head twitching the view, while still reaching the side windows with a real look.
- **Mind your lighting.** TrackIR (IR) shrugs off room light; the Tobii and webcam-based OpenTrack prefer consistent, non-backlit lighting on your face.
- **Disable USB selective suspend** in Windows so the camera does not drop out mid-flight — the same fix that cures a lot of panel dropouts.
- **Pair it with sensible camera settings.** Head tracking exposes how your default cockpit view is framed; spend five minutes setting a good neutral seat position first.
- **Plan the camera into your station.** If you are building a full setup, position the monitor and camera so your neutral seat is consistent every session — map it out alongside the desk in [the Rig Configurator](/racing/configurator).

## Who should skip it

Skip head tracking entirely if you fly in **VR** — the headset already tracks your head, so a TrackIR or Tobii does nothing for you. Skip the **Tobii** specifically if you fly mostly fighters and aerobatics and want the sharpest response; the cheaper TrackIR is the better tool there. And skip the name-brand options if you are on a real budget and enjoy tinkering — **OpenTrack** gets you 90% of the experience for pocket change. Finally, if you fly exclusively airliners locked onto the autopilot and the PFD, you may simply not look around enough to justify any of it.

## Verdict

For most MSFS 2024 simmers on a monitor, the **Tobii Eye Tracker 5** (~$299-339) is the one I recommend first: it is native, hands-free, and the slight responsiveness gap does not matter for GA and airliner flying, which is what most people actually do. If you fly fast and aggressive — warbirds, fighters, aerobatics — the **TrackIR 5** (~$150) is both cheaper and sharper, and the clip is a fair trade for that precision. And if budget is the deciding factor, **OpenTrack** delivers real 6DOF for almost nothing if you are willing to set it up. Whichever you pick, head tracking is the rare upgrade that costs less than a throttle quadrant and changes more about how the sim feels.
