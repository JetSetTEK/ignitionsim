Verdict first: **for the three platforms most home builds run — GSPro, E6 Connect, and Foresight FSX — you need a Windows PC with a dedicated NVIDIA GPU, but a roughly $1,100 mid-range build clears 1080p on all of them.** You do not need an RTX 4090, an i9, or 64 GB of RAM. And if you would rather not own a PC at all, one unit — the Garmin Approach R50 — runs a full simulator on its own touchscreen and skips the entire question.

It is the debate that splits every simulator build thread in two: do you need a gaming PC, or can you get by with the laptop already on your desk? The honest answer sits in between, and the nuance matters because the wrong call costs you either a frustrating stutter on every drive or a few hundred dollars you did not need to spend. The good news for 2026 is that the bar has come down — the days of needing an i9 and a $1,600 graphics card to run a sim are over.

Here is what the major software actually demands, where you can stop spending, and who can skip the gaming PC entirely.

## The short answer

For **GSPro**, **E6 Connect**, and **Foresight FSX**, you need a **Windows PC with a dedicated GPU**. Integrated graphics — the chip built into most laptop CPUs — will not run them. You do *not* need a flagship rig; a mid-range build handles all three at 1080p, which is the resolution most projectors render anyway. The lone software exception is the lightweight Awesome Golf, and the lone hardware escape hatch is an all-in-one like the R50 that carries its own simulator.

If you have not locked in your software yet, do that first — it changes the spec target. Our [golf simulator software comparison](/golf/best-golf-simulator-software) covers which platforms work with which launch monitors, because compatibility can quietly eliminate a sensor before the PC question even comes up.

## The real spec floor by software and resolution

These are the current 2026 requirements pulled from each platform's own documentation. Prices and firmware move, so treat the tiers below as the floor, not a promise — always check the current spec page before you buy parts.

| Software | GPU (1080p) | CPU | RAM | Storage / Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| GSPro | RTX 3060 / 4060 (RX 6600 / 6700 XT also listed) | Intel i5 / Ryzen 5 | 16 GB | ~10 GB min; **each course ≈ 1 GB**. 4K Ultra wants RTX 4070 / 5070+ and 32 GB |
| E6 Connect | GTX 1070 min; RTX 3060 for smooth 1080p | Intel i5 (i7 recommended) | 8 GB (16 GB+ recommended) | Also runs on iPad/iOS via the E6 Connect app |
| FSX Play | RTX 3060 min; 3070 Ti / 3080 / 3090 better | 7th-gen Intel or newer | 8 GB (16 GB with Swing Camera) | **AMD GPUs not supported; AMD CPUs not recommended; NVIDIA Quadro not supported** |

Two things jump out. First, the GPU floor for smooth play across all three is roughly an **RTX 3060-class card** — that is a genuinely mid-range part now, not an enthusiast splurge. (E6's *minimum* dips to a GTX 1070, but community consensus still lands on a 3060 for a comfortable 60+ fps.) Second, **FSX is the strict one**: it locks out AMD graphics entirely and discourages AMD processors. If a Foresight or Bushnell unit running FSX is anywhere in your plans, **build Intel + NVIDIA** and do not gamble on a Ryzen-plus-Radeon machine.

One 2026 wrinkle worth knowing: GSPro does not natively support DLSS, FSR, or frame generation, so an RTX 50-series card's headline upscaling feature is not accessible in the sim. A 50-series card still works fine — you just are not buying it for the frame-gen trick.

## Decision tree: do you actually need a PC?

Work top to bottom and stop at the first line that fits.

1. **Want GSPro's full course library, E6, or FSX?** → Yes, you need a Windows PC with a dedicated NVIDIA GPU. Continue to the build section.
2. **Running a Foresight or Bushnell unit on FSX?** → Build **Intel + NVIDIA**. AMD is off the table.
3. **Fine with an all-in-one and want zero PC?** → A **Garmin Approach R50** runs a full simulator on its own screen. Skip the PC line.
4. **Just want data and casual play, on a budget, no PC?** → A **Garmin R10** plays Home Tee Hero from a paired phone or tablet — no gaming rig, but not a bottomless PC course library either.
5. **Only ever practicing, not playing courses?** → A launch monitor's bundled practice app (SkyTrak's range, for example) may cover you with no GSPro subscription or gaming PC at all.

## The laptop tax

You can absolutely run a sim on a gaming laptop, but understand the trade. A laptop GPU delivers only about **70 to 80 percent of its desktop-named equivalent** because of power and thermal limits, so GSPro explicitly recommends stepping up one GPU tier on a laptop to hit the same frame rate — a laptop "RTX 4070" performs closer to a desktop 4060. More importantly, you cannot upgrade a laptop's GPU later. For a permanent bay tucked behind the enclosure, a desktop is more future-proof and more cost-effective per frame. A laptop only earns its place if portability — carrying the sim brain to a friend's bay, say — is a genuine requirement.

## Where you can stop spending

- **You do not need an RTX 4090 or 5090.** Nothing in home sim software at 1080p comes close to taxing it.
- **You do not need an i9.** An i5 / Ryzen 5 feeds these GPUs fine; the sim engines are GPU-bound, not CPU-bound, at typical resolutions.
- **You do not need 64 GB of RAM.** 16 GB runs 1080p; 32 GB is comfortable insurance for 4K, swing cameras, and background launch-monitor apps.
- **You do not need liquid cooling or RGB anything.** This is a bay PC, not a benchmark queen.

## Who can skip the gaming PC entirely

There is a real out for some buyers. The **Garmin Approach R50** (~$4,999) runs a full built-in simulator on its own 10-inch touchscreen with **no external PC at all** — Home Tee Hero, 43,000+ courses, review video, and full virtual rounds, powered by the unit's own processor. Reviewers report it going from powered-off to a first tee shot in well under a minute, and a single HDMI cable pushes the image to a projector or TV when you are ready to build a bay. The entry **Garmin R10** (~$600) also plays Home Tee Hero with no gaming PC, but it runs the simulation on a **paired phone or tablet** rather than fully on-device, so it is a lighter, screen-dependent experience.

The tradeoff is course depth and openness: an all-in-one gives you Garmin's ecosystem and setup simplicity, while a PC platform like GSPro gives you a bottomless, community-fed course library and connector flexibility. If your goal is data and casual play rather than deep course libraries, skipping the PC is a legitimate — if pricier and shallower — path. ## The build-first buy order

1. **Lock your software first.** GSPro, E6, or FSX — the choice sets your GPU floor and your AMD-or-not rule.
2. **Match the GPU tier to your projector's resolution**, not to a benchmark chart. 1080p projector → RTX 3060/4060/5060 class. 4K premium screen → RTX 4070/5070 class.
3. **Buy Intel + NVIDIA if FSX is anywhere in your future.** It is the cheapest insurance in the whole build.
4. **Buy a 1 TB NVMe SSD minimum.** Courses are ~1 GB each and a small drive becomes a chore.
5. **16 GB of RAM for 1080p, 32 GB if you run 4K, swing cameras, or a launch-monitor app alongside.**
6. **Stop there.** No i9, no 4090, no liquid cooling. Put the savings into the mat, screen, and projector — the parts you actually feel.

## The verdict

You need a Windows PC with a dedicated NVIDIA GPU, and a roughly $1,100 build with an RTX 5060-class card, 16 GB of RAM, and a 1 TB SSD runs every major platform at 1080p with room to spare. Spend more only for 4K. Build Intel + NVIDIA if FSX is anywhere in your future, and do not let a laptop's thermals or a small SSD undercut an otherwise sound build. And if you would rather not own a PC at all, an all-in-one like the R50 is a legitimate — if pricier and shallower — way out.
