Verdict first: **for most new pilots the [WinWing Ursa Minor](https://winwingsim.com/) pair (~$220-260) is the beginner HOSAS to buy** — magnetic sensors, real feel, in-stick haptics, and an ecosystem to grow into, all under $300. Buy **twin [Thrustmaster T.16000M](/space/gear/thrustmaster-t16000m-fcs) sticks** (~$150-160) if you're not yet sure you'll stick with two-stick flying, and buy the **[VKB Gladiator NXT EVO](/space/gear/vkb-gladiator-nxt-evo-space-combat-edition) pair** (~$240-300) if you already know you'll climb to a boutique endgame someday. There is no wrong answer in this list — only the question of how committed you already are, and one trap the spec sheets hide: **the off-hand variant is the one that's perpetually out of stock, so buy both sticks in the same cart.**

The single most expensive mistake a new space pilot makes is assuming a real two-stick rig requires boutique money. It does not. You can build a working **HOSAS** — Hands On Stick And Stick — for under $300, and for many pilots that rig is the *last* rig they ever need. If you're still deciding between two sticks and a stick-plus-throttle, read [HOSAS vs HOTAS](/space/hosas-vs-hotas) first — this guide assumes you've already decided two sticks is the goal.

## The shortlist at a glance

| Setup | Pair price | Sensors | Vibration | Left-hand ready | Upgrade path |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Thrustmaster T.16000M** ×2 | ~$150-160 | Hall-effect | No | Fully mappable | Dead end (replace) |
| **WinWing Ursa Minor** ×2 | ~$220-260 | Magnetic | Yes (in-stick) | Yes | Into WinWing Orion 2 |
| **VKB Gladiator NXT EVO** ×2 | ~$240-300 | Magnetic (contactless) | No | Yes | Into VKB Gunfighter Mk.IV |

All three clear the one spec that actually prevents stick drift — contactless or Hall-effect sensors on the main axes — so none of them wears into the center wander that plagues cheap potentiometer sticks. The differences are feel, build, and what you can do *after* the first rig. Everything else is preference.

## Tier 1: Twin Thrustmaster T.16000M — the ~$150 on-ramp

Two **Thrustmaster T.16000M** sticks land around $150-160, or roughly $150 as the **T.16000M FCS Space Sim Duo** twin pack. This is the canonical cheap HOSAS, and the reason is one feature: the stick is genuinely ambidextrous and **fully left-hand mappable**, so you can flip one for your off hand without buying a special grip.

The sensors are **Hall-effect** on the main pitch and roll axes, which means no carbon-track potentiometers to wear into drift on those axes. The build is plastic and light. Sixteen buttons, a hat, and a twist axis per stick give you enough analog and digital inputs to cover all six ship axes with room left over.

What you give up: it feels like an ~$80 stick because it is one. The springs are light, the twist is a little vague, and there is no path to upgrade — when you outgrow it, you replace it, you don't extend it. That's fine. As a first rig that proves whether you even like two-stick flying before spending real money, nothing beats it.

**Who should skip this:** anyone who already knows they want boutique feel and is willing to wait. Buying twin T.16000Ms and then twin Gladiators six months later costs more than just buying the Gladiators.

## Tier 2: WinWing Ursa Minor pair — the budget champion

A pair of **WinWing Ursa Minor** sticks runs around $220-260. WinWing reframed the budget conversation in 2024-2025 by undercutting the boutiques while adding things they don't have. You get **magnetic sensors** on X/Y, a space-friendly button layout, a twist axis on the Space variant, and — uniquely at this price — an **in-stick vibration motor** that buzzes on hits and thruster events. It's gimmicky on paper and genuinely good in practice for feedback you'd otherwise miss.

Configured through WinWing's **SimAppPro** software, the Ursa Minor pair is the easiest "real" recommendation under $300 for a pilot who wants to skip the disposable tier entirely. And it sits inside an ecosystem — if you later want a metal mid-tier base, WinWing's **Orion 2** line is the step up, so the Ursa Minor is not a dead end the way the T.16000M is. The trade-offs are honest: the gimbal leans on glass-fiber-reinforced nylon rather than the all-metal internals of VKB, and SimAppPro is consistently called the weakest part of the package.

**Who should skip this:** pilots who specifically want the VKB gimbal pedigree or plan to climb the VKB ladder to a Gunfighter. Different ecosystem, different long game.

## Tier 3: VKB Gladiator NXT EVO pair — the upgrade-path play

Two **VKB Gladiator NXT EVO "Space Combat Edition"** sticks (around $119-139 each) land between $240 and $300 depending on grips and shipping. This is the most-recommended boutique on-ramp, and the reason is not the stick alone — it's the ladder behind it.

The Gladiator uses VKB's contactless magnetic gimbal in an all-metal mechanism that feels a clear tier above the budget plastic, plus a self-centering twist axis — the "sixth axis" that lets a twin-stick pair cover all six degrees of freedom. It builds as either a dedicated left or right hand for a true mirrored pair. And critically, when you outgrow it you don't throw it away — you step the grips onto a **Gunfighter Mk.IV** base for adjustable cams, swappable springs, and independent axis dampers. That trajectory is the whole point of buying VKB at the entry level.

The cost: VKB's **VKBDevCfg** tuning software is powerful and intimidating, and the Gladiators sometimes sell out — grips routinely go out of stock for weeks. You're buying a platform, not just a controller.

## What about a Logitech HOTAS instead?

New pilots searching for a cheap space setup keep landing on the **Logitech X52** or **X56** — and it's worth being clear about why they're not in this ranking. Both are single-box **HOTAS** units (one twisting stick plus an integrated throttle), not a HOSAS twin-stick pair. The twist axis reaches yaw, and they're comfortable, complete, plug-and-play on-ramps for someone who wants one box. But you can't mirror a right-hand HOTAS stick onto your off hand, so you can't build a true two-stick rig out of one — and their potentiometer-era sticks are more drift-prone than the magnetic and Hall-effect sensors above.

If your heart is set on a one-box HOTAS to start, that's a legitimate different path — read [HOSAS vs HOTAS](/space/hosas-vs-hotas) to decide. But if you want a *HOSAS* under $300, buy two of one ambidextrous stick from the tiers above, not a Logitech HOTAS.

## How to actually choose

Map it to your honesty about your own commitment:

- **Testing the waters?** Twin T.16000M. Lowest risk, lowest spend, and Hall-effect sensors so it won't drift while you learn.
- **Know you're staying, want it to feel good now?** Ursa Minor pair. Best value-to-feel ratio under $300, with haptics nothing else offers this cheap.
- **Know you'll chase the high end eventually?** Gladiator pair. Buy the ladder, not the controller.

Whatever you pick, budget for one thing this guide's prices don't include: **mounting**. Two loose sticks slide under combat input, and a Monstertech-style clamp arm is the standard fix. A HOSAS needs two.

## The four traps nobody warns beginners about

Buying the sticks is the easy part. Four things trip up new HOSAS pilots more than any sensor spec:

<div class="callout warning">
  <div class="ct">// Trap 1 — binding six axes is the real setup cost</div>
  <p>Star Citizen has well over 200 possible binds; Elite Dangerous is friendlier but still asks you to map six axes across two sticks by hand. The hardware is plug-and-play; the <em>binding</em> is an evening's work. Budget that evening, and consider a labeled macro panel (an Elgato Stream Deck) once you outgrow memorizing button clusters — it pairs with any of these sticks equally.</p>
</div>

<div class="callout warning">
  <div class="ct">// Trap 2 — desk space is not optional</div>
  <p>A HOSAS is two bases plus two mounts plus your keyboard and mouse space. Pilots who buy the sticks and discover their desk can't fit both at a comfortable, symmetric width end up hunched or cramped. Measure your desk before you buy, and plan for two Monstertech-style clamp arms — a HOSAS needs a wider, more deliberate footprint than a single HOTAS.</p>
</div>

<div class="callout warning">
  <div class="ct">// Trap 3 — you need an ambidextrous or left-mappable stick</div>
  <p>This is the make-or-break spec for a beginner HOSAS. Only a stick that's genuinely ambidextrous (or fully left-hand mappable, like the T.16000M) can serve as your off hand. A right-hand-only stick — or a single-box HOTAS — cannot be mirrored, which is exactly why the Logitech X52/X56 don't make this list. Buy the correct left/right variants, not two of the same hand.</p>
</div>

<div class="callout warning">
  <div class="ct">// Trap 4 — plan the upgrade path before you spend twice</div>
  <p>The T.16000M is a dead end by design — a great $150 proof of concept, but you replace it, not extend it. The Ursa Minor and Gladiator both build into ecosystems (WinWing Orion 2, VKB Gunfighter). If you already know you'll climb, buying the cheap sticks first and the boutique sticks six months later costs more than just buying the boutique sticks once. Match your first purchase to your honest commitment level.</p>
</div>

## The buy order

1. **Decide your commitment level honestly** — testing, staying, or climbing. That single answer picks your tier.
2. **Buy both sticks in the same cart** — HOSAS sticks ship as dedicated left- or right-hand units, and the off-hand variant is the one that's perpetually out of stock.
3. **Add two Monstertech mounts to that same cart** — light bases slide under combat input; a HOSAS needs one per stick.
4. **Skip the throttle** — the off-hand stick already covers lateral thrust. Add one later only if you want set-and-forget cruise.
5. **Budget an evening to bind six axes** — and add a Stream Deck later if the button count outgrows your memory, not before you own the sticks.

## Verdict

For most new pilots the **WinWing Ursa Minor pair** at ~$220-260 is the sweet spot: magnetic sensors, real feel, in-stick vibration, and an ecosystem to grow into, all under $300. Buy **twin T.16000Ms** (~$150-160) if you're not yet sure you'll stick with two-stick flying — the Hall-effect sensors mean it won't drift while you learn — and buy the **VKB Gladiator pair** (~$240-300) if you already know you want to climb to a Gunfighter someday. Skip the Logitech X52/X56 for a HOSAS: they're one-box HOTAS units that can't be mirrored onto an off hand. There is no wrong answer in this list — only the question of how committed you already are. When you're ready to settle the closest call in the lineup, I put the two budget magnetic sticks head-to-head in [Ursa Minor vs Gladiator NXT EVO](/space/winwing-ursa-minor-vs-vkb-gladiator-nxt-evo), and I map all three boutique brands in [VKB vs Virpil vs WinWing](/space/vkb-vs-virpil-vs-winwing-space-sticks-2026).
