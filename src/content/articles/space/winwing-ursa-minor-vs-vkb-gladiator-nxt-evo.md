## The Ursa Minor's case: feedback and value

WinWing crossed over from building DCS-grade cockpit replicas, and the Ursa Minor Space is its budget-champion play. It does two things the Gladiator doesn't.

First, **in-stick vibration**. There's a motor in the grip head that buzzes on hits and thruster events, synced to in-game state. On paper it reads like a gamepad gimmick. In a cockpit it's the cheapest immersion-per-dollar in the category — you *feel* incoming fire and shield breaks a beat before your eyes catch the meter. It's easy to disable if it annoys you, and most pilots leave it on. The one asterisk: enabling the vibration motor and lighting requires WinWing's SimAppPro software, which is also the stick's weakest link (more on that below). The stick flies plug-and-play; the haptics do not.

Second, **price and features-per-dollar**. The Ursa Minor Space typically undercuts the Gladiator per stick — community reviews consistently land it around $110, or roughly $99 in Europe — while packing four hat switches, a mini-stick, and 40-plus inputs onto a space-friendly layout. There's a genuine, recurring talking point in the community that WinWing built "the same mechanics as VKB for a couple hundred euros less," and while that's a marketing-flavored simplification, the magnetic-sensor parity underneath it is real. For a pilot who wants a real magnetic-sensor rig without boutique pricing, the Ursa Minor lowers the money barrier.

The trade-offs are honest ones. The gimbal leans on glass-fiber-reinforced nylon rather than the Gladiator's all-metal internals, so it feels lighter and less planted under aggressive input — reviewers call it responsive and solid, but not metal-solid. SimAppPro draws consistent criticism as primitive, and at least one reviewer flagged a phone-number registration wall to unlock features. And there are scattered quality-control reports — a mushy hat here, a slightly loose button there — the kind of thing you accept at this price but should know about. WinWing's upgrade path also runs into the Orion 2 base line, a real ecosystem but a narrower and less storied one than VKB's gimbal ladder.

## The Gladiator's case: pedigree and the ladder

The **VKB Gladiator NXT EVO "Space Combat Edition"** is the boutique-adjacent benchmark that everything cheap gets measured against. Its all-metal-internals gimbal is a clear tier of mechanical feel above budget plastic, and it runs contactless MaRS magnetic sensors on X/Y plus a **lockable** twist axis — a small detail that matters more than it sounds. Many pilots lock twist out on the aiming stick to prevent accidental yaw while keeping it live on the off-hand stick as a translation axis. The Ursa Minor's twist doesn't lock in hardware the same way. And it's fully plug-and-play for basic flight, with VKBDevCfg an optional tool you open only when you want to tune curves and deadzones.

But the real argument for the Gladiator isn't the stick — it's what's *behind* it. The Gladiator is the bottom rung of a ladder. Fly it, love the magnetic-sensor feel, and when you outgrow the fixed cam-and-spring feel you step the same grips onto a [Gunfighter Mk.IV base](/space/gear/vkb-gunfighter-mk4-base) (~$289, base only) with adjustable cams, swappable springs, independent axis dampers, and a no-stiction soft-start clutch — then top it with an [MCG Pro grip](/space/gear/vkb-mcg-pro-grip) (~$136) for six analog axes of on-stick control. That trajectory — Gladiator pair, then a tunable base, then a heavier grip — is the single strongest reason to buy VKB at the entry level, and I map the whole spend in [the VKB Gladiator-to-Gunfighter upgrade path](/space/vkb-gladiator-to-gunfighter-upgrade-path).

The cost of that pedigree runs two ways. Per stick, the Gladiator is usually a touch pricier than the Ursa Minor. And VKBDevCfg, while optional, is powerful and intimidating when you do open it — deadzone defaults want tuning, curves apply across the full axis range independent of the deadzone, and the panel assumes you'll read documentation. It rewards the effort with finer control than SimAppPro, but it asks for that effort up front.

## Where the gap actually shows

For the first hundred hours, honestly, you'd be hard-pressed to tell these apart in a blind test of pure aiming performance. Both have the sensors. Both fly fine. The gap shows up at two specific moments:

- **In your first firefight**, where the Ursa Minor's vibration motor tells you you're taking damage a beat before your eyes catch the shield meter. Point: WinWing.
- **At the eighteen-month mark**, when you want more, and the VKB pilot bolts a Gunfighter base under grips they already own while the WinWing pilot is shopping a whole new line. Point: VKB.

That's the trade in one sentence: the Ursa Minor is the better stick *today*, the Gladiator is the better *investment*.

## The setup tax nobody prices in

Whichever stick you pick, two costs sit outside the stick's price tag — and they hit a HOSAS harder than a single-stick rig.

Budget the mounts when you budget the sticks, not after your first firefight sends a stick skating.</p>

## The bind overflow — the fix both sticks share

Neither the Ursa Minor nor the Gladiator has enough buttons for a fully-bound Star Citizen ship, and no budget stick does. The community answer, regardless of which you run, is a labeled macro panel: the [Elgato Stream Deck XL](/space/gear/elgato-stream-deck-xl) (~$250; the 15-key MK.2 is ~$150) turns 32 icon-driven LCD keys into a visible panel for power triage, weapon groups, shield pips, landing gear, and comms, with folders for effectively unlimited binds. It pairs with either stick equally — this is the one purchase in your build that's brand-agnostic, and it's a bigger quality-of-life jump for a new pilot than any gimbal upgrade.

## The buy order

1. **Confirm two sticks is your layout** — if you're not sure, read [HOSAS vs HOTAS](/space/hosas-vs-hotas) before spending a dollar.
2. **Pick your side of the trade** — vibration-and-value now (Ursa Minor) or the upgrade ladder later (Gladiator). Both clear the magnetic-sensor bar, so there's no wrong answer.
3. **Buy a dedicated left and a dedicated right** — never two of the same handed grip. Both lines offer L and R; confirm handedness at checkout.
4. **Add two Monstertech mounts in the same cart** — light bases slide under combat input, and a HOSAS needs both hands rigid.
5. **Add a Stream Deck** (or the cheaper MK.2) before you chase a fancier base — the 200-plus binds hurt more than a spring feel.
6. **Budget an evening** for SimAppPro or VKBDevCfg before your first serious flight.
7. **Only then** consider climbing the VKB ladder to a Gunfighter tier — if you went VKB and you now know exactly what feel you want to change.

## Verdict

Buy the **WinWing Ursa Minor Space** pair if you want the best feel-and-feedback for the money right now and you're not sure you'll ever chase the high end — the vibration motor is real immersion, it's usually the cheaper pair, and its magnetic sensors clear the same bar as the VKB. Just go in knowing SimAppPro is rough and the gimbal is plastic. Buy the **VKB Gladiator NXT EVO Space Combat Edition** pair if you already suspect you'll climb to a Gunfighter someday, because buying the bottom of a ladder you intend to climb beats buying a stick you'll later replace wholesale — and the all-metal gimbal and lockable twist are nicer to live with in the meantime.

Either way you're getting a legitimate magnetic-sensor HOSAS for well under $300 a pair, which a few years ago would have been impossible.
