Buy an aluminum clamp arm per stick and the problem disappears. **[Monstertech](https://www.mtsim.com/product/joystick-hotas-table-mount/) is the proven universal standard** (~$129 per desk mount, widest brand compatibility, quick-release clamp) and the safe default. **[Foxx Mount](https://foxxmount.com/) is the cheaper US-made modular alternative** if you want to spend less and don't mind a smaller compatibility list. **[Virpil's VPC Desk Mount V4](https://virpil-controls.us.com/vpc-desk-mount-v4-l.html) is the steel matched-set option** if you already fly Virpil bases. A HOSAS needs *two* mounts — budget them with the sticks, not after your first firefight sends one skating across the desk.

Nobody warns you about this part. You research sensors, you debate [VKB versus Virpil versus WinWing](/space/vkb-vs-virpil-vs-winwing-space-sticks-2026), you finally build your two-stick rig — and then in your first real dogfight one of the sticks slowly walks across your desk while you're trying to put rounds on a fighter. You weren't aiming badly. Your hardware was *moving*.

This is the mounting problem, and it's unique to **HOSAS**. A single stick or a gamepad mostly takes vertical and twisting forces. Two sticks under combat input take *lateral* forces — and lateral forces are exactly what a baseplate resting on a desk has no defense against. If you've already got your binds dialed in via [binding all six axes across a HOSAS setup](/space/how-to-bind-six-axes-hosas), this is the next thing standing between you and steady aim.

## Why baseplates slide (the physics)

A stick sitting on a desk resists sliding with exactly two things: friction against the surface, and its own weight pressing down. That's it.

When you slam a stick to its corner to roll hard or bleed lateral strafe onto a target, you apply a force that's mostly *sideways*. The friction holding the base in place has to exceed that sideways force, and it usually can't — especially with a light stick on a smooth desk. So the base creeps. A millimeter per hard input. Over a ten-minute fight, the stick has wandered out of its ergonomic position and your aim has drifted with it, and you blame the curve tuning when the real culprit is Newton.

| Surface / mount | Resists slide? | Lets you set angles? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bare desk | Poorly | No | Baseline; slides under combat input |
| Desk + rubber feet / mat | Better | No | Helps light sticks; still creeps over time |
| Heavy metal base on desk | Moderately | No | Weight buys you time, not immunity |
| Desk-clamp aluminum arm | **Fully** | Yes | Bolts to base, clamps to desk edge |
| Chair-mounted arm | **Fully** | Yes | Most ergonomic; moves with you |

Weight and rubber help. They do not solve it. The only thing that *removes* slide is taking the lateral force out of the friction equation entirely — by bolting the stick to something that isn't going anywhere.

## The fix: clamp the sticks to rigid furniture

That's what an aluminum clamp arm does. The stick's base bolts to the arm; the arm clamps to your desk edge or chair frame. Now when you slam the stick, the lateral force feeds into the desk, not into a friction joint that's about to lose. The stick does not move. Ever.

The arms also do a second thing that matters more than people expect: they let you **set the angle** — and drop the sticks lower. Two sticks flat on a desk force your wrists into an unnatural splay at desktop height. Mounted on arms, you can cant each stick inward, tilt it back, and lower it toward your lap so your wrists sit neutral, which is the difference between a forty-minute session and an hour-six session. Reviewers who've lived with these mounts consistently make the same point: dropped below desk height and center-mounted rather than off to the side, the placement starts to feel like a real cockpit and your brain stops fighting the throw angles.

## Desk mount or chair mount?

Both work. The choice is ergonomic preference.

**Desk-edge clamps** are simpler, cheaper to start with, and don't care what chair you own. The sticks live at the front edge of your desk (or dropped just below it) and you reach to them. The downside: if you push back from the desk, your sticks don't come with you. The other catch reviewers flag is knee clearance — arms that hang below the desk edge mean you approach your chair more carefully so you don't bark a shin on aluminum.

**Chair-arm mounts** clamp to the armrest anchor points of your gaming chair so the sticks sit in your lap zone at natural angles and *move with you* when you recline or shift. This is the more immersive option and the one many pilots gravitate to once they've felt it — it's as close to a cockpit as a home setup gets. The catch is your chair's armrests have to physically accept the clamp plates; Monstertech ships several armrest-specific forms, but not every chair works.

There's a temptation to think mounting is an enthusiast concern — that if you bought twin **Thrustmaster T.16000M** sticks you can skip it. The opposite is true. Light plastic sticks slide *more* than heavy metal ones, so a budget HOSAS benefits from mounting even more than a VKB or Virpil rig. Even a heavy [Virpil WarBRD-D](/space/gear/virpil-warbrd-d-base) base creeps under hard input eventually; a light T.16000M base walks in minutes.

## The setup checklist

When you mount, get these right:

- **Bolt pattern.** Confirm your stick's base matches the arm's mounting plate or that an adapter exists. Monstertech covers most major sticks with universal plates; Foxx Mount and Virpil sell device-specific plates — check *your* exact stick before you buy.
- **Clamp depth.** Measure your desk edge or chair armrest thickness against the clamp's range. Monstertech's BESSEY clamp fits roughly 0.5&ndash;10cm; Virpil's V4 fits 9&ndash;63mm. Too thick and it won't close; too thin and it wobbles.
- **Angle and height, then lock.** Set the cant, tilt, and drop to your wrists at rest, not your wrists reaching. Lower and center-mounted usually beats high and off to the side. Lock everything down hard — a half-tight arm flexes and you're back to a moving stick.
- **Knee clearance.** If the arms hang below the desk edge, note where they sit so you don't collide with them sitting down.
- **Cable routing.** Mounted sticks move with the chair or sit at the desk edge; leave slack so a recline doesn't yank a USB cable.

<div class="callout warning">
  <div class="ct">// Warning — a half-tight arm is worse than no arm</div>
  <p>The most common mounting mistake isn't buying the wrong brand — it's under-tightening. An arm that isn't fully locked flexes under combat input, which reintroduces exactly the wandering you bought the mount to kill, plus a new rattle. Torque every joint down hard, then push the stick to its corners and confirm nothing gives. If you feel flex, it isn't tight yet.</p>

## Where the mount sits in the buy order

Mounting isn't a late-game upgrade. It slots in right beside the sticks:

1. **Pick your sticks first** — budget T.16000M pair, boutique VKB/Virpil/WinWing, whatever fits your [HOSAS budget](/space/best-beginner-hosas-under-300).
2. **Buy two mounts in the same cart** — one per stick. Monstertech for universal safety, Foxx Mount to save money, Virpil V4 if you're all-Virpil.
3. **Choose desk or chair** — chair mounts if your armrests take them and you want lap-zone immersion; desk clamps if not.
4. **Set angle, height, and lock** before your first serious flight — lowered and canted inward, wrists neutral.
5. **Only then** chase deadzone and curve tuning — a mounted stick makes those adjustments actually stick.

## Verdict

Mounting isn't an upgrade you earn later — it's part of a HOSAS rig the same way the second stick is. Baseplates slide because lateral combat forces beat friction, and the only real fix is clamping each stick to rigid furniture so the force has somewhere to go. **Buy Monstertech if you want the proven universal standard, Foxx Mount if you want to save money on a US-made modular kit, and Virpil's VPC Desk Mount V4 if you're already all-Virpil.** Choose chair mounts if your armrests take them and desk clamps if they don't, mount the sticks *low* and *canted inward* to your wrists, and don't kid yourself that a budget rig is exempt — light sticks slide the most. Get this right and you remove a whole category of "why is my aim drifting" frustration that has nothing to do with your binds or your curves. For the binding and tuning side of steady aim, see [how to bind all six axes across a HOSAS setup](/space/how-to-bind-six-axes-hosas); for the sticks themselves, [the three-brand boutique breakdown](/space/vkb-vs-virpil-vs-winwing-space-sticks-2026).
