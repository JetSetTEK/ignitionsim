It's a "match the rig to the mission" guide.

## For combat and FA-off: HOSAS wins, decisively

If you fight — bounty hunting, conflict zones, PvP, or you just love flying with flight-assist off — two analog sticks are the answer. Elite gives spacecraft full six-degrees-of-freedom movement: three rotation axes (pitch, yaw, roll) plus three translation axes (forward/back, up/down, lateral strafe). A single stick plus throttle physically can't reach all of those as smooth analog inputs at once. A **HOSAS** setup puts rotation on the right stick and translation thrust on the left, so you can strafe sideways while pitching and rolling — the movement patterns that wreck opponents who can only turn.

The budget entry is two **Thrustmaster T.16000M** sticks. Buy them as the **Space Sim Duo** twin-pack (~$120) and you get two identical, fully ambidextrous Hall-effect sticks that reconfigure into a left and right hand — the canonical cheap HOSAS. Step up to a **VKB Gladiator NXT EVO Space Combat Edition** pair (~$119-139 per stick) for contactless magnetic sensors, a self-centering twist axis, and a genuine upgrade path into VKB's ecosystem. Either way, the deep skill is binding all six axes cleanly — our [six-axis HOSAS binding guide](/space/how-to-bind-six-axes-hosas) walks through exactly which axis goes where, including why most pilots disable twist on the aiming stick.

## For exploration and trading: keep the throttle

Here's where the HOSAS evangelists overreach, and where Elite genuinely diverges from Star Citizen. If your Elite life is 6,000 light-years out in the black — honking the discovery scanner, managing fuel scoops, holding a cruise speed for hours — you do not need lateral strafe. You need a comfortable, precise throttle axis you can park at 75% and forget. A spring-centered second stick is actively worse for that. This is why plenty of veteran Elite pilots fly **HOTAS** and never apologize for it: Elite's flight model leans on pitch/yaw/roll plus a held forward throttle, and a detented lever maps that cleanly for supercruise and docking.

On a budget, the **Thrustmaster T.16000M FCS HOTAS** (~$170) is the smart pick: the same Hall-effect stick paired with the TWCS throttle, which slides on a linear rail like a real fighter throttle instead of rotating. As a bonus, you can later buy a second T.16000M stick and convert toward a hybrid rig. The popular step-up is the **Logitech X56** — a real dual-lever throttle with a wall of switches for Elite's long bind list — but as noted above, stock has been intermittent and we're keeping it text-only with no hard price. For the deeper HOTAS-family conversation, our [HOSAS vs HOTAS explainer](/space/hosas-vs-hotas) breaks down exactly which axes each layout reaches.

## The Operations wrinkle: keep a gamepad on the desk

The 2026 **Operations** update, live since late June, is the first genuinely new Elite buying consideration in a while. It adds squad-based, multi-stage missions for up to four commanders, split across Mercenary and Powerplay modes, with cooperative combat that runs **both in space and on foot** — start with a ship firefight, then fight on foot as the mission escalates. Frontier has pitched megaship interiors and zero-gravity scenarios as part of the direction; the exact scope of what shipped is worth confirming in the current patch notes, since sources described it differently in the run-up.

Here's the practical point for a controls buyer: **a HOSAS is miserable for FPS movement.** So whatever you fly with, the 2026 move is to keep a **gamepad within arm's reach** for the on-foot stretches — or mouse and keyboard if you prefer FPS precision. It's the cheapest buying consideration in this whole guide: a ~$60 controller you probably already own.

## The honest budget floor: a gamepad

Don't let the boutique-stick crowd shame you. A plain **gamepad** flies Elite Dangerous perfectly well, handles the new on-foot content natively, and costs ~$60. It's less precise than a HOSAS in a dogfight and it can't match a throttle for cruise control, but it's the cheapest viable entry and it's genuinely good for relaxed PvE flying — and after Operations, it's the *only* controller that does the whole loop, ship and foot, out of one device.

**Who should skip a flight stick entirely:** couch players, casual PvE pilots, and anyone who values the seamless ship-to-foot transition Operations leans into. Start on a gamepad, decide later if a stick tempts you.

## Xbox players: your options are narrow

On Xbox, boutique sticks (VKB, Virpil, WinWing) simply don't work — they're PC-only. The **Turtle Beach VelocityOne Flightstick** (~$130, seen ~$97-99 on sale) is the main licensed Xbox flight stick, with Hall-effect axes, an OLED data display, an integrated mini-throttle and 27 programmable buttons. It's a capable single stick; the common complaints are a stiff, non-adjustable centering spring, small throttle levers, and a light base that tips. If you're on Xbox and want more than a gamepad, it's essentially your one real choice — so we're linking it to search and to Turtle Beach's official page rather than embedding an unverified product photo. The stick is the easy part. Three things trip up new Elite pilots more than any gimbal spec — budget for them when you budget the sticks.

Elite has more functions than any stick has buttons — power triage, weapon groups, shield pips, landing gear, cargo scoop, comms, fuel scoop, and now the on-foot loadout on top. No stick solves this in hardware. The community answer, whatever you fly, is a labeled macro panel: the **Elgato Stream Deck XL** turns 32 icon-driven LCD keys into a visible panel with folders for effectively unlimited binds, so you stop memorizing unmarked switches. It pairs with a HOSAS, a HOTAS, or a gamepad equally — this is the one purchase that's setup-agnostic, and it's a bigger quality-of-life jump for a new Elite pilot than any gimbal upgrade.

## The buy order

1. **Pick your config by your main activity, not the loudest forum.** Combat / FA-off → HOSAS. Exploration / trading → HOTAS. Xbox → the licensed VelocityOne. Do everything → a gamepad does the whole loop.
2. **Start cheap either way.** Twin T.16000Ms for a first HOSAS (~$120 the Duo), or the T.16000M FCS HOTAS (~$170) for a first stick-and-throttle. Prove you'll stick with the hobby before boutique spend.
3. **If HOSAS, buy ambidextrous-twice or a handed L+R pair** — never two of the same handed grip.
4. **Budget two Monstertech mounts with the sticks** — light bases slide, and a HOSAS needs both hands rigid.
5. **Add a Stream Deck for the bind overflow** before you chase a fancier base — Elite's bind list hurts more than a spring feel.
6. **Keep a gamepad on the desk** for the Operations on-foot fights, on a separate control profile.
7. **Only then climb** to a VKB Gunfighter or Virpil tier — once you know exactly what feel you want to change.

## Verdict

The **best controls for Elite Dangerous in 2026** depend on your loop, and Elite is the rare space sim where that answer really flips. Fight or fly FA-off and you want **HOSAS** — twin T.16000M to start at ~$120, a VKB Gladiator pair to commit. Explore or trade and a **HOTAS** with a real throttle is more comfortable for the long haul, and Elite's flight model rewards the lever.
